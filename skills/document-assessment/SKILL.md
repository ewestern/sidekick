## When this knowledge applies

When routing documents for deeper processing, when summarizing a document and
needing to know where significance tends to live, or when assessing whether a
document is likely to contain newsworthy content.

## Domain knowledge

### The lifeblood principle

Potter on the government beat: "Documents are the lifeblood of government."
Government produces enormous volumes of records, most of which are routine.
The skill is quickly identifying which documents contain decisions, disclosures,
or anomalies — and where within those documents the significant content lives.

The Data Journalism Handbook's "Five Minute Field Guide" describes a systematic
rapid-assessment approach: before deep analysis, first understand what type of
document you have and what questions it is designed to answer.

### Document type taxonomy

**Agenda** — what the body intends to discuss and vote on at an upcoming meeting
- Significance level: *medium* (future decisions, not yet made)
- Key sections: non-consent items, staff recommendation summaries, public
  hearing notices, items pulled from consent

**Minutes** — the official record of what actually happened at a meeting
- Significance level: *high* (official record of decisions made)
- Key sections: vote tallies on each action item, public comment summaries,
  motions and their language, any items tabled or continued

**Ordinance** — a law enacted by the governing body
- Significance level: *high* (binding rule change)
- Key sections: the enacting clause ("the council hereby ordains"), the
  effective date, any superseded provisions, definitions section

**Resolution** — a formal decision that is not a law
- Significance level: *medium-high* (formal approval or expression of intent)
- Key sections: the "whereas" clauses (context), the "resolved" clauses
  (the actual decision), the vote recorded at adoption

**Staff report** — prepared by staff to inform a vote
- Significance level: *medium* (analysis, not decision)
- Key sections: executive summary, recommendation, background/history, fiscal
  impact, alternatives considered, public comments received

**Budget document** — proposed or adopted allocation of public funds
- Significance level: *high* (controls all spending)
- Key sections: executive summary, fund summaries, departmental allocations,
  year-over-year change tables, capital improvement plan

**Audit / financial statement** — independent review of accounts
- Significance level: *high* (accountability for public money)
- Key sections: auditor's opinion letter (clean, qualified, adverse?),
  management discussion and analysis, notes to financial statements, findings

**Contract** — a binding agreement for goods or services
- Significance level: *medium-high* (commitment of public money)
- Key sections: parties, term, total value, scope of work, amendment history

**Permit / entitlement** — authorization for a specific project or activity
- Significance level: *medium* (applies to specific property/activity)
- Key sections: what is being authorized, conditions of approval, any
  environmental findings, expiration date

### What raises significance within a document type

Within any document type, certain features elevate significance:

- **Contested action**: a vote with dissents, especially 3-2 or 4-3 splits
- **Dollar threshold**: any single expenditure, contract, or budget line
  above a material threshold (varies by jurisdiction, but typically >$100K
  warrants attention in smaller agencies)
- **Public opposition**: public comment section shows organized opposition
- **Reversal or amendment**: amends, rescinds, or continues a prior action
- **Legal finding**: includes findings of fact required by law (CEQA,
  zoning findings, financial necessity determinations)
- **Emergency designation**: any action taken under emergency powers
- **Waiver of normal procedure**: any motion to waive competitive bidding,
  environmental review, or public notice requirements

### Routine vs. substantive — practical distinctions

**Routine** (low expected significance):
- Consent calendar items approved without discussion
- Routine renewals of existing contracts without material changes
- Acknowledgment of reports without action
- Procedural motions (continuation, adjournment)
- Approval of prior meeting minutes

**Substantive** (higher expected significance):
- New contracts, especially sole-source awards
- Any item pulled from consent for separate discussion
- Any action with a fiscal note above threshold
- Any amendment to existing policy or ordinance
- Any hearing on a contested permit or variance

### The Data Journalism Handbook's systematic approach

Before diving into a document, note:
1. What type is this? (Sets expectations for what it contains)
2. What time period does it cover? (Sets context for comparison)
3. Who produced it? (Indicates perspective and completeness)
4. Is this the authoritative version? (Drafts and finals differ)
5. What questions can this document answer? (Focus on purpose-fit)

## Gotchas

**Consent calendars hide important items.** Items placed on the consent calendar
are approved as a block without individual discussion — but their content may
be substantive. A sole-source contract on the consent calendar is still a sole-
source contract.

**Draft vs. approved versions differ.** Staff reports are revised between
publication and the meeting. Minutes may differ from contemporaneous notes.
Always note which version is being processed.

**Length ≠ importance.** A two-paragraph resolution adopting a $50 million
budget is more significant than a 40-page environmental impact report on a
minor project.

**Absence of conflict ≠ absence of significance.** A unanimous vote on a
major policy change may be significant even without dissent. Track what was
decided, not just whether anyone disagreed.
