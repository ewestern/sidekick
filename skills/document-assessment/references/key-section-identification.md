# Key Section Identification

Where to focus analytical attention within each common government document type. Documents are often long; not all sections carry equal informational value.

---

## Meeting agenda

**Read first**: Items listed as "Public Hearings" and "New Business." These are where contested decisions live.

**Read second**: The list of consent calendar items. Scan for: dollar amounts, sole-source designations, contract amendments, and any item with a fiscal note. Consent items are approved as a block — their content may be substantive even if their handling is routine.

**Read third**: Staff recommendation summaries for each non-consent item. The recommendation tells you what staff thinks should happen; the council may disagree.

**Skip or skim**: call to order, flag salute, roll call, approval of prior minutes, and ceremonial presentations (unless the presentation itself is the story).

**Note**: items added after the initial agenda posting (often called "late add" or "supplemental agenda") warrant extra attention — the normal 72-hour public notice did not apply to them.

---

## Meeting minutes

**Read first**: The vote record for each action item. For each vote: who moved, who seconded, how each member voted, and whether any member abstained or was absent.

**Read second**: Any public comment summary. Look for organized opposition, expert testimony, or public comment that contradicts the staff report.

**Read third**: Motions made and their exact language. A motion to "approve with conditions" differs from a motion to "approve as recommended." The exact language matters for ordinances and resolutions.

**Read fourth**: Items that were tabled, continued to a future meeting, or referred to a committee — these are unresolved, not dismissed.

**Skip or skim**: boilerplate call to order, roll call, and adjournment. The approval of prior minutes (unless there was a dispute about them).

---

## Ordinance

**Read first**: The enacting clause — "the council hereby ordains…" — and the operative sections that follow. This is the actual legal text that takes effect.

**Read second**: The effective date. Urgency ordinances take effect immediately; standard ordinances typically take effect 30 days after adoption.

**Read third**: Any superseded provisions — "Section X of Ordinance 2024-08 is hereby amended to read…" — tells you what changed and from what.

**Read fourth**: The definitions section, if present. Definitions control how the operative sections apply.

**Skip or skim**: "whereas" recitals are context, not law. They explain the rationale but do not have legal force.

---

## Resolution

**Read first**: The "resolved" clauses — typically numbered — which constitute the formal decision. Each "resolved" clause is a discrete action.

**Read second**: The vote recorded at adoption (if included in the resolution itself; otherwise it will be in the minutes).

**Read third**: The "whereas" clauses, which provide context: what problem the resolution is addressing, what prior actions it relates to, and what authority it relies on.

**Key distinction**: resolutions are formal but not law. A resolution "urging the state legislature to act" expresses intent but creates no binding obligation. A resolution "authorizing the city manager to enter into a contract" creates a real obligation.

---

## Staff report

**Read first**: The executive summary — typically one to three paragraphs at the top. This is staff's distillation of what matters.

**Read second**: The staff recommendation — a specific action proposed for council vote. Note whether it is conditional ("staff recommends approval, subject to conditions of approval listed in Exhibit A").

**Read third**: The fiscal impact section. Any expenditure commitment, budget adjustment, or long-term financial obligation is here.

**Read fourth**: The alternatives section. If staff presents only one option, that is worth noting. If multiple options are presented, the alternatives section reveals the options staff rejected and why.

**Read fifth**: Any public comments received — from agencies, neighbors, or organized groups — before the meeting.

**Skip or skim**: background and history sections are useful context but are typically what staff chose to emphasize. Long technical appendices can usually be reviewed only if the summary raises a specific question.

---

## Budget document

**Read first**: The executive summary or budget message. This is where the controlling priorities and the headline numbers live — but read it knowing it is a persuasive document written to justify the budget.

**Read second**: The fund summary table — total revenues, total expenditures, and any use of reserves. If expenditures exceed revenues and the difference is covered by a reserve draw, that is a significant fact.

**Read third**: Year-over-year comparison tables by department. Where did appropriations increase significantly? Where did they decrease? Position counts (FTEs) are a leading indicator of service-level changes.

**Read fourth**: The capital improvement plan — multi-year project list. Check for projects added, removed, or deferred from prior years.

**Skip or skim**: detailed line-item schedules within departments unless a specific department or line item is the subject of investigation.

---

## Audit / financial statement

**Read first**: The auditor's opinion letter (usually near the front). The key language:
- "Unqualified opinion" or "clean opinion" — no material issues found
- "Qualified opinion" — material issues found but financial statements are otherwise fairly presented
- "Adverse opinion" — financial statements are not fairly presented
- "Disclaimer of opinion" — auditors could not gather sufficient evidence

**Read second**: The management letter or internal control findings section. This is where the auditor lists weaknesses in how the agency manages its money, even when the overall opinion is clean.

**Read third**: Notes to financial statements. Pension and OPEB liability disclosures, contingent liabilities, pending litigation, and significant accounting policy changes are buried here.

**Read fourth**: The management discussion and analysis (MD&A) section — staff's narrative about financial results. Read knowing this is management's perspective; compare to the actual numbers.

**Skip or skim**: individual fund schedules and supplementary information unless a specific fund or program is the focus.

---

## Contract

**Read first**: Parties — who is the contractor, and is the contracting party what was described in the award resolution? (The actual legal entity may differ from the trade name.)

**Read second**: Total value, payment terms, and term length. Note whether this is a not-to-exceed figure or a guaranteed payment.

**Read third**: Scope of work. Is it specific enough to be enforceable? Vague scopes are a flag for potential disputes or unauthorized expanded work.

**Read fourth**: Amendment history if visible. Multiple amendments may indicate scope creep, performance problems, or a relationship that has expanded beyond what was originally approved.

**Skip or skim**: standard boilerplate indemnification, insurance, and general conditions clauses unless there is a specific legal question about them.
