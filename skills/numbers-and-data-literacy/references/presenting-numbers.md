# Presenting Numbers

Methods for translating raw figures into audience-accessible context without distorting meaning.

---

## The core principle: anchor to something the audience already knows

A raw number — $4.7 million, 12,000 residents, 847 incidents — means little without a reference point. The goal is to find an anchor that gives the number scale and significance without implying more precision or meaning than the evidence supports.

**Good anchors**:
- A prior year's figure (year-over-year context)
- A comparable jurisdiction or unit (peer context)
- A familiar unit of human experience (per household, per day, per person)
- A concrete equivalent (the cost of X public employees for a year; X miles of road)

**Bad anchors**:
- Made-up comparisons that sound plausible but are not documented
- Comparisons to national figures when local figures exist
- Anchors that imply a judgment ("only $4 million" vs. "as much as $4 million")

---

## Translation techniques

### Per-unit translation

Divide a large aggregate figure by the number of relevant units:

- Total cost ÷ population = per-capita cost
- Total budget ÷ households = per-household impact
- Total contract value ÷ years = annual cost
- Penalty amount ÷ number of violations = per-violation amount
- Total savings ÷ residents = savings per resident

**Example**: A $2.4 million parks budget sounds large in isolation.
- Per-capita (city of 40,000): $60 per resident per year
- Per day: about $6,575 per day to operate the entire parks system
- Per household: roughly $100 per household per year if there are 24,000 households

Each translation is accurate; choose the one that is most meaningful for the specific story.

### Rate of change framing

Instead of raw amounts, describe the direction and scale of change:

- "The parks budget grew by $240,000, or 10%, from last year"
- "In inflation-adjusted terms, the city is spending 8% less per resident on parks than it did in 2015"
- "The contract is 35% larger than the agency's previous agreement with the same firm"

Rate of change framing is most useful when the trend is the story.

### Time-based translation

Divide an annual figure by time:

- Annual contract: per month, per week, or per day cost
- Annual budget: per-day cost of operating a department
- One-time expenditure: amortized over useful life if relevant

**Useful for**: making large annual figures concrete, or making a daily/hourly cost relatable.
- "The city's $600,000 annual contract with the consulting firm works out to about $1,600 per day"
- A $10 million road project amortized over 20 years: "$500,000 per year"

### Comparison to something concrete and known

For very large or very small figures, anchor to something within the audience's common knowledge:

- "The cost of one police officer's annual salary and benefits, typically around $120,000–$150,000 in this region"
- "Roughly the cost of repaving one mile of city street"
- "The equivalent of about 5% of the city's annual parks maintenance budget"

**Caution**: the anchor itself must be accurate and should be confirmed, not approximated. "About the cost of a police officer" requires knowing what a police officer actually costs.

## Rounding conventions

Raw figures in government documents are often carried to the penny but should not be reported that way unless precision is specifically the point.

| Magnitude | Round to |
|---|---|
| Under $1,000 | Nearest $10 or $100 |
| $1,000 – $10,000 | Nearest $100 or $1,000 |
| $10,000 – $1,000,000 | Nearest $1,000 or nearest $10,000 |
| $1,000,000 – $1,000,000,000 | "About $X million" or "$X.X million" |
| Over $1 billion | "About $X billion" or "$X.X billion" |

**When precision matters**: exact figures are worth preserving when they reveal something (a contract that came in at exactly the bid threshold; a budget adjustment of exactly the amount needed to cover a deficit). In those cases, note the precision explicitly.

## Framing without editorializing

Numbers can be framed to imply more than they prove. Some framing choices are invisible (they seem neutral but carry a perspective):

| Framing | Implication |
|---|---|
| "Only $4 million" | Small; not worth worrying about |
| "As much as $4 million" | Large; potentially excessive |
| "Up to $4 million" | An upper bound; actual may be less |
| "At least $4 million" | A lower bound; could be more |
| "$4 million" | Neutral; just the figure |

The neutral presentation is almost always preferable unless the framing is explicitly the editorial judgment.

---

## When to present raw vs. contextualized numbers

**Raw is appropriate when**:
- The exact figure is the point (the contract amount was $X)
- The document's wording matters for accuracy
- The reader needs the precise figure for their own calculation

**Contextualized is appropriate when**:
- The significance of the number is not self-evident
- Comparison across time or jurisdiction is the point
- The audience needs to understand what the number means for them personally

For most coverage of government financial decisions, both are useful: the raw figure for accuracy, the contextualized figure for meaning.

---

## Avoiding false precision in estimates

When a figure is an estimate, projection, or model output, avoid implying that it is as precise as an audited actual:

- "The city estimates the project will cost between $8 million and $12 million" (not "$9.7 million")
- "The administration projected revenues of about $45 million, compared to the prior year's actual of $43.2 million"
- "Based on the agency's methodology, the unfunded pension liability is approximately $180 million as of the last actuarial valuation"

Range estimates, qualifier language ("approximately," "an estimated," "projected"), and explicit citation of the source and date of the estimate maintain accuracy while avoiding false precision.
