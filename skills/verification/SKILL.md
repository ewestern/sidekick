## When this knowledge applies

When assessing how much confidence to place in a claim, figure, name, vote
tally, or other piece of information encountered in a document or transcript.
Also applies when surfacing information that a downstream agent or editor
will need to verify before publication.

## Domain knowledge

### Credibility is the asset, accuracy is how you protect it

Joseph Pulitzer's rule: "Accuracy, accuracy, and accuracy." A study of errors
at the Portland Oregonian found they happened mainly because of:
- Working from memory
- Making assumptions
- Dealing with second-hand sources

Each of these has a direct parallel in document processing: assuming a
number is correct without checking the arithmetic, treating a press release
as authoritative, or carrying forward a claim from a summary without
returning to the source document.

### The spectrum from unverified to corroborated

Information lives on a spectrum:

| Level | What it means |
|-------|---------------|
| Assertion | One source made this claim |
| Internally consistent | The claim is coherent with other information in the same document |
| Corroborated | The claim appears in multiple independent sources |
| Documented | The claim is supported by a primary source document |
| Established fact | The claim is indisputable and does not need attribution |

Most information from a single government document sits at "assertion" or
"internally consistent." Corroboration requires checking against other
documents or sources. Established facts (the vote occurred, the meeting took
place, the ordinance number is what it is) can be stated without attribution.

### Primary vs. secondary sources

A **primary source** has direct knowledge: the person who made the decision,
the official record of a vote, the signed contract, the meeting minutes signed
by the clerk. A **secondary source** is derived from or summarizes a primary
source: a press release, a news report, an unofficial summary.

Secondary sources are most useful as a way of confirming information already
found in primary sources — not as standalone evidence. A city's own press
release summarizing a council vote is less reliable than the official minutes.

### Internal consistency checks

Before treating a number or claim as accurate, check:
- Do the figures add up? (Does the total match the sum of its parts?)
- Is the timeline coherent? (Does this date follow that date?)
- Does the role/title match other references to the same person?
- Is the ordinance or resolution number consistent across references?

Government documents contain transcription errors, version conflicts, and
copied-forward mistakes. A budget document may contain subtotals that do not
match line items. Meeting minutes may record a vote differently than the
approved resolution.

### What does not need verification

Reporters' three rules: do not working from memory, do not assume, seek primary
sources. But over-attribution makes stories unreadable. Information a reporter
(or agent) directly observed — or that is indisputably established — can be
stated without hedging. The distinction is:

- **Observed facts**: what happened in the document (the vote tally is in the
  minutes) — state directly
- **Interpretations**: what this vote means for the community — these need
  grounding, not just assertion
- **Attributable claims**: what someone said or decided — needs attribution
  to the speaker or decision-maker

## Gotchas

**Press releases are secondary sources.** A city's press release describing a
decision is not the same as the official record. Press releases include only
facts that reflect positively on the issuing agency. Always prefer the
underlying record (minutes, resolution, ordinance) when available.

**A confident assertion is not a corroborated fact.** An official stating
something firmly does not make it accurate. Public officials sometimes
misremember, misstate, or strategically misrepresent. The underlying document
is the check.

**"The document says X" and "X is true" are different claims.** A document may
contain errors, outdated information, or contested figures. Reporting what a
document says requires attribution; reporting an established fact does not.

**Cross-document discrepancies are themselves newsworthy.** When two official
documents from the same government body contradict each other on the same
fact, the discrepancy is significant — not just an error to reconcile.
