## When this knowledge applies

When interpreting government meeting documents (agendas, minutes, staff
reports, ordinances, resolutions), when determining whether an action taken
at a meeting is routine or significant, or when characterizing what a vote
or motion means.

## Domain knowledge

### Meeting anatomy

A standard local government meeting follows a predictable structure. Knowing
the structure tells you where to look for news.

**Call to order / Roll call**: establishes a quorum. If quorum is absent, no
binding action can be taken. Note: a quorum is typically a majority of
the full body, not just those present.

**Public comment (general)**: residents may speak on any topic not on the
agenda. Useful for identifying emerging issues; not itself a decision point.

**Consent calendar**: a block of items approved together without individual
discussion. Designed for routine, uncontested matters. Any member may pull
an item from consent for separate discussion — if this happens, note it.
Items on the consent calendar are approved unless pulled; their content may
be substantive regardless of how they are handled procedurally.

**Public hearings**: items that require opportunity for the public to comment
before a vote. Legally required for most land use decisions, many rate
increases, and other matters specified by statute. The record of public
testimony is legally significant and is part of the basis for any findings.

**Old business**: items previously introduced that are continuing.

**New business**: new items presented for the first time. The most common
location for significant votes.

**Closed session / Executive session**: portion of the meeting from which the
public is excluded. Permissible topics are specified by state law (typically:
pending litigation, personnel matters, real property negotiations). Actions
taken in closed session must generally be reported in open session.

**Adjournment**: meeting ends. Sometimes followed by an immediately
subsequent special meeting on a narrow topic.

### Motions and voting

**Making a motion**: a member proposes an action: "I move that the council
approve staff's recommendation." Without a motion, no vote can be taken.

**Seconding**: another member must second the motion before discussion or
a vote. A second does not indicate support — it only means the member thinks
the matter warrants discussion.

**Discussion**: members may debate the motion before voting. Amendments to
the motion can be made at this stage (and require a second).

**Vote**: typically voice vote (aye/nay), show of hands, or roll call.
- **Roll call vote**: required for ordinances in many states; produces a named
  record of each member's vote.
- **Abstention**: a member declines to vote. In many jurisdictions, an
  abstaining member is treated as neither aye nor nay and their absence from
  the tally may affect the majority count.
- **Absence**: different from abstention. An absent member did not vote.

**Majority types**:
- *Simple majority*: more than half of members voting (most routine actions)
- *Supermajority*: two-thirds or three-quarters required (overriding a veto,
  urgency ordinances, condemnation)
- *Majority of full membership*: a majority of all seated members, regardless
  of who is present — required for some actions even if that creates a de
  facto supermajority of those present

**Failed motions**: a motion that fails dies without action. The body has
declined to act, not acted to deny. The distinction matters: "the council
failed to approve" ≠ "the council rejected."

### The consent calendar — where news hides

Items placed on consent are approved as a block without individual
discussion. Staff determines what belongs on consent; common items include:
- Routine contract renewals
- Approval of prior meeting minutes
- Receipt of reports without action
- Standard resolutions

What does not belong on consent, but sometimes appears there:
- Sole-source contracts above competitive bidding threshold
- Contracts with vendors who have political connections
- Waivers of normal procedural requirements
- Amendments that materially change prior agreements

Reporters and beat agents should scan consent calendars item by item.

### Distinguishing procedural from substantive actions

| Procedural | Substantive |
|---|---|
| Approval of prior meeting minutes | Adoption of an ordinance or resolution |
| Motion to continue an item | Vote on a contested permit |
| Appointment of a subcommittee | Contract award |
| Setting a public hearing date | Budget adoption |
| Adjournment | Emergency declaration |

Procedural actions keep the meeting functioning; substantive actions change
something in the world outside the meeting. The test: "If this action had
gone the other way, would anything be different?"

### Parliamentary procedure essentials (Robert's Rules)

Most local government bodies formally adopt Robert's Rules of Order or a
simplified version. Key concepts:

**Quorum**: the minimum number of members required for the body to take
binding action. Typically a simple majority of filled seats.

**Point of order**: a member may raise a point of order if they believe
procedure is being violated. The chair rules on it; the ruling can be
appealed to the full body.

**Motion to table / postpone indefinitely**: postpones an item without
scheduling its return. Different from a continuance (which sets a new date).
An item tabled indefinitely has been effectively, if not formally, killed.

**Motion to reconsider**: allows a previously decided item to be brought up
again. Usually must be made by a member who voted on the prevailing side.

**Substitute motion**: replaces the original motion with a different one.
The substitute must be voted on first; if it passes, it becomes the pending
motion.

### What makes an agenda item newsworthy

Not all council business is news. The most reliably newsworthy items are those that:

- Allocate or redirect public money above a material threshold
- Grant or deny a permit, license, or contract after contested proceedings
- Change a policy or law that affects many residents
- Override, delay, or contradict a prior decision
- Produce a contested vote after public opposition
- Were pulled from consent for separate discussion (the pull is itself a signal)
- Involve a waiver of normal competitive bidding, environmental review, or
  public notice requirements
- Involve an urgency or emergency designation

## Gotchas

**A failed motion ≠ a decision to deny.** The council failing to pass a
motion to approve is procedurally different from voting to deny. In some
cases, a developer can immediately refile after a failed approval motion;
in others, they cannot. The procedural outcome matters.

**Closed session announcements are required.** Before entering closed
session, the body must announce the topic (litigation, personnel, etc.)
in open session. The topic is public even if the deliberation is not.

**Special meetings have limited agendas.** A special meeting can only address
items listed on its agenda — no additional items can be added or taken up.
Regular meetings have more flexibility.

**Urgency ordinances require a supermajority.** Standard ordinances take
effect 30 days after adoption. Urgency ordinances take effect immediately
but require a two-thirds or four-fifths vote in most states — and the urgency
finding must be stated on the record.

**Abstentions in land use cases may be legally treated as approvals.** In some
states, a planning commissioner who abstains on a discretionary permit is
treated as approving it for voting purposes. Check local rules.
