# News Values Framework: Local Government Application

Expanded definitions of each news value with examples drawn from local government coverage.

---

## Timeliness

**Core definition**: The event just happened, or we just learned about it.

**In local government coverage**:
- A council vote taken last night is timely today
- A budget adopted two weeks ago is still timely if no coverage has appeared
- A contract signed quietly three months ago becomes timely when a complaint is filed
- A FOIA response received today may be timely even if the underlying records are years old

**Timeliness traps**:
- Assuming that because something happened at a scheduled meeting it is already covered — check
- Assuming that old records lack news value — an old contract can be newly timely
- Confusing timeliness with urgency: a soft-news piece may be timely without urgency

---

## Impact

**Core definition**: Many people are affected, or the effect on some is very large.

**Scaling heuristics for local government**:

| Action type | Impact signal |
|---|---|
| Water/sewer rate change | Every ratepayer in the service area |
| Zoning reclassification | Adjacent property owners + prospective developers |
| School closure | Enrolled families + staff + neighborhood |
| Street closure or traffic change | Affected commuters; businesses |
| Single contract award | Primarily financial accountability, not direct service impact |
| Staff reorganization | Limited unless it affects a service the public relies on |

**Emotional impact modifier**: An action affecting children, housing security, or public safety carries emotional weight disproportionate to raw numbers. A school closure affecting 200 families may be more newsworthy than a $10 million procurement.

**Long-tail impact**: Budget cuts often have delayed, distributed impact. The cut is visible; the impact materializes over years. Note this distinction in framing.

---

## Proximity

**Core definition**: It happened here, or involves people from here.

**In local government coverage**: proximity is always present — everything is local. The more useful question is *differential proximity*:

- Which neighborhoods are most directly affected?
- Are effects concentrated in one part of the jurisdiction or distributed?
- Does this affect renters, homeowners, or both?
- Are effects concentrated in a demographic group the coverage area should be tracking?

**Proximity signals worth noting**:
- A zoning change in one neighborhood that sets precedent across the city
- A contract with a vendor from outside the jurisdiction vs. a local business
- A policy change that mirrors what a neighboring city did (cross-jurisdiction proximity)

---

## Controversy

**Core definition**: Meaningful disagreement exists — about the decision, its process, or its likely effects.

**Forms of controversy in government documents**:

| Form | Signal |
|---|---|
| Split vote | Any 4-3, 3-2, or closer on a governing body |
| Public opposition | Organized testimony or written comments against an item |
| Policy reversal | Amends, rescinds, or contradicts a prior decision |
| Contested procedure | Motions to waive competitive bidding, public notice, or review requirements |
| Staff/council disagreement | Staff recommends denial; council approves anyway (or vice versa) |
| Appeal or legal challenge | Filed after a decision |

**Absence of controversy ≠ absence of significance**: A unanimous vote on a major budget commitment is still significant even without dissent. Track what was decided, not just whether anyone disagreed.

---

## Prominence

**Core definition**: A well-known person or institution is involved.

**In local government context — prominence is relative**:

Prominence should be assessed against the relevant coverage scope, not a national standard.

| Level | Example |
|---|---|
| Prominent (jurisdiction-wide) | Mayor, city council members, school board trustees |
| Prominent (beat-level) | Developer with multiple prior applications before the planning commission |
| Prominent (issue-level) | Neighborhood association leader who has appeared at six consecutive hearings |
| Not prominent | First-time applicant; individual member of the public |

**Institutional prominence**: The institution can be prominent even when the individual is not. A contract with a locally recognized firm, a grant from a well-known foundation, or a decision by the state legislature affecting the city all carry institutional prominence.

**Track role, not just name**: "City attorney" is a prominent role even across personnel changes.

---

## Currency

**Core definition**: People are already talking about this; it connects to an ongoing public conversation.

**Currency accelerators in local government coverage**:
- Active state or federal legislation that the local decision relates to
- Recent media coverage that created awareness of an issue
- A neighborhood meeting or petition that preceded the government action
- A similar controversy in a neighboring jurisdiction that has received attention
- An election or campaign where this issue was debated

**Currency is independent of timeliness**: A three-year-old zoning application becomes newly current when a neighborhood meeting erupts over it this week. Currency is about what the audience is currently thinking, not when something happened.

**Currency degrades**: An issue that dominated coverage six months ago may no longer have currency unless something new has occurred.

---

## Oddity

**Core definition**: What happened is unusual, unexpected, or inconsistent with established pattern.

**Oddity signals in government documents**:

| Observation | Why it's odd |
|---|---|
| 4-3 vote on a consent item | Consent items are routinely unanimous |
| Emergency ordinance passage | Standard procedure has a 30-day minimum |
| Sole-source award above threshold without documented justification | Normal competitive bidding was waived |
| Required quarterly report not filed | Agency has a mandatory obligation |
| Contract amended more than twice | Unusual even for complex projects |
| Same contractor on consecutive no-bid awards | Pattern suggests something beyond coincidence |
| Staff recommends denial; commission overrules unanimously | Suggests political rather than technical consideration |

**Oddity as investigation signal**: A single odd item may be a minor error. Oddity that recurs, or that clusters around a specific person, organization, or time period, is an investigation signal.

---

## Applying multiple values

News value accumulates across criteria. An item that scores on one criterion is a brief or a mention. An item that scores on four or five is a story.

**Example assessment — sole-source contract award on consent calendar**:
- Timeliness: voted on last night ✓
- Impact: $400,000 commitment of public funds ✓
- Proximity: local agency ✓
- Controversy: no public opposition noted, but…
- Oddity: waiver of competitive bidding ✓ ; placed on consent without individual discussion ✓
- Prominence: contractor has received two prior contracts this year ✓

Five criteria satisfied → warrants a story, not just a brief.
