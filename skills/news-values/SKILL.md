## When this knowledge applies

When assessing whether information from a document, meeting, or event is
significant enough to surface for editorial attention, or when determining the
relative importance of competing story angles.

## Domain knowledge

### The seven news values

Journalists weigh information against seven established criteria. These are not
a checklist — they are factors that accumulate. An item that scores on multiple
values is more newsworthy than one that scores on only one.

**Timeliness** — Did this happen recently, or did we just learn about it?
Recency matters, but timeliness for a pipeline processing government documents
is relative: a budget adopted two weeks ago is still timely if coverage hasn't
caught up.

**Impact** — Are many people affected, or just a few? Broad impact is
automatic for city-wide decisions (water rates, zoning changes, school closures)
but must be assessed for more narrow actions (a single contract award, a staff
reorganization). Also includes emotional impact: a decision affecting children
or housing carries more weight than raw numbers suggest.

**Proximity** — Did this happen here, or involve people from here? Local
government coverage has built-in proximity — everything is local. The question
shifts to: which neighborhoods or populations are most directly affected?

**Controversy** — Are people in disagreement? Contested votes, public
opposition, split council decisions, or policy reversals all carry controversy
value. Controversy does not require personal conflict — a contested ordinance or
disputed budget line item qualifies.

**Prominence** — Is a well-known person or institution involved? Elected
officials carry prominence by office. A developer with a long record before
the council is prominent in context. A first-time applicant is not.

**Currency** — Are people already talking about this? A government decision
gains currency when it connects to an ongoing public conversation (housing
affordability, school funding, public safety). Currency can transform a routine
action into a significant story.

**Oddity** — Is what happened unusual or unexpected? A unanimous vote is
routine; a 4-3 split on an item that appeared uncontroversial is odd. An
agency that never misses a deadline suddenly failing to file a required report
is odd. Oddity is often a signal of underlying conflict or dysfunction.

### Hard news vs. soft news

**Hard news** is timely, consequential, and actionable — the audience needs it
now because it affects their lives or decisions. Government votes, budget
adoptions, policy changes, regulatory actions: these are hard news.

**Soft news** (features) does not have a mandatory publication date. A profile
of a long-serving council member, a trend piece on neighborhood change — these
are soft news. They may have the same ultimate importance but they can wait.

The distinction matters because it determines urgency, not significance. A
soft-news story about homelessness trends may ultimately be more important than
a hard-news brief about a zoning variance, but the zoning vote has a deadline
the trend story does not.

### News from different sources

Journalists encounter news through three channels:
- **Naturally occurring events**: disasters, accidents, unexpected announcements
- **Planned activities**: scheduled meetings, hearings, report releases — the
  backbone of local government coverage
- **Reporter enterprise**: stories found by following up, analyzing records,
  or noticing patterns others missed

For this pipeline, planned activities (meeting agendas, scheduled filings) and
enterprise (patterns across documents over time) are the primary channels.

### Applying news values to local government

The most reliably newsworthy government actions are those that:
- Allocate or redirect public money
- Grant or deny a permit, license, or contract
- Change a policy that affects many residents
- Override, delay, or contradict a prior decision
- Produce a contested vote after public opposition

Routine consent-calendar items, procedural votes, and administrative
housekeeping rarely satisfy multiple news values and generally do not warrant
flagging unless something anomalous occurs within them.

## Gotchas

**Currency ≠ timeliness.** Currency is about what the community is discussing,
not when something happened. A three-year-old zoning application becomes
newly current when a neighborhood meeting erupts over it.

**"Balance" is not the same as fairness.** Giving equal prominence to a
unanimous scientific or financial consensus and a fringe dissenting position
is misleading, not balanced. Fairness means representing all significant
viewpoints proportionally — not mechanically equally.

**Prominence is contextual.** A local developer who appears repeatedly in
zoning decisions is prominent on that beat, even if unknown statewide.
Prominence should be assessed within the relevant coverage scope, not
against a national standard.

**Absence can be newsworthy.** A document that was expected and didn't arrive,
a report that was due and wasn't filed, a vote that was scheduled and
postponed — these can be as significant as what was actually produced.
