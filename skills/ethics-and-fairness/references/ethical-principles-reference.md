# Ethical Principles Reference

Extended treatment of accuracy, fairness, independence, and accountability with local government examples.

---

## Accuracy

**The standard**: every factual claim is verifiable; errors are corrected promptly and prominently.

**Why it matters**: Potter cites a study of errors at the Portland Oregonian — errors happened primarily because reporters worked from memory, made assumptions, or relied on second-hand sources. Each has an analog in document processing: carrying forward a figure from a summary without verifying it against the source document; assuming a quote is accurate without checking the transcript; treating a press release as the authoritative record.

**Accuracy in local government coverage**:

- Vote tallies can be wrong in unofficial summaries; verify against the official minutes
- Dollar figures get rounded, misread, or transposed; verify against the source document
- Names are misspelled in staff reports; verify against official rosters
- Titles change; verify that a person's current title matches what is in the document
- Dates can be off by a day, a month, or a year; verify against the authoritative document

**Accuracy of impression**: a story can be technically accurate in every individual fact but convey a misleading overall impression. Selecting only facts that support one conclusion, while omitting equally documented facts that complicate it, produces technically accurate but substantively dishonest coverage. Accuracy of impression requires that the facts selected and emphasized represent the full picture, not just the most convenient subset.

**Corrections**: when an error is discovered, correct it promptly and specifically. Not "an earlier version of this article contained an error" — but "an earlier version misstated the vote tally as 4-3; the correct tally was 5-2." The correction should be as prominent as the error it corrects.

## Fairness

**The standard**: all parties with significant stakes have an opportunity to respond; their positions are represented proportionally and with fidelity to what they actually said.

**What fairness requires in local government coverage**:

- Before publishing a critical finding about an official, agency, or applicant, they must have a meaningful opportunity to respond — not a pro forma last-minute question, but a genuine chance to review the specific allegations and provide their account
- When multiple positions exist on a contested matter, each is represented in proportion to its significance — not mechanically equally
- The position of an organized majority of public commenters is represented as a majority position, not as "some residents"
- Staff's recommendation is distinguished from the council's decision when they differ

**Fairness ≠ balance in the mechanical sense**: a unanimous scientific or financial consensus and a single dissenting opinion do not deserve equal treatment. Fairness means representing all significant viewpoints proportionally — not giving a fringe position equal prominence to a well-documented consensus.

**Fairness in framing**: using charged language ("scheme," "cronies," "rubber stamp") without evidentiary basis is unfair to the subjects. Use neutral, specific language and let the facts carry the weight. "The council approved a $3 million no-bid contract to a firm whose principal had donated to the mayor's campaign" is specific and attributable. "The council's cronyism continued" is an editorial judgment presented as fact.

**The right of response in accountability coverage**: when a story may damage someone's reputation or career, the obligation to seek response is highest. Their response — including any refusal to comment — belongs in the story.

## Independence

**The standard**: coverage is driven by evidence and public interest, not by the interests of the entities being covered, the publication's financial relationships, or the preferences of officials.

**Independence threats in local government coverage**:

- **Access journalism**: a reporter or publication that relies on official sources for access has an incentive to avoid negative coverage of those sources. The relationship becomes transactional. Independence requires willingness to report accurately even when it damages a relationship.
- **Official framing**: government agencies set the terms of public debate through press releases, press conferences, and exclusive briefings. Accepting the agency's framing — its characterization of a policy as a "success," its description of a decision as "fiscally responsible" — is a loss of independence.
- **Institutional alliances**: a publication whose major advertisers are real estate developers has an institutional incentive to cover development favorably. This does not mean coverage will be biased — but the incentive must be recognized and resisted.

**Independence for automated systems**: an AI pipeline can have systematic biases embedded in its training, prompting, or data sources. Independence for a pipeline means examining its systematic outputs, not just individual decisions, for patterns of emphasis or omission.

**The independence test**: if the findings pointed in the opposite direction — against a favored source, favorable to a disfavored one — would the same coverage decisions be made? If not, independence has been compromised.

## Accountability

**The standard**: hold power responsible for its use of public authority and public money.

**Why accountability journalism exists**: government makes consequential decisions in the public's name, using public resources. The public has a right to know how those resources are used and whether officials are acting consistent with the public interest. No other institution systematically performs this function at the local level.

**What accountability requires**:

- Covering not just what officials say but what they do, and whether the two are consistent
- Following up: a story that raises a question without following it to its resolution is incomplete
- Covering failure and dysfunction, not just successful programs and press releases
- Protecting the access to public records and public proceedings that make accountability possible

**Proportionality in accountability coverage**: accountability journalism has significant consequences for the individuals covered. The evidentiary standard should be proportional to the severity of the allegation. Documenting inefficiency requires less evidentiary confidence than alleging corruption. Both may be worth reporting; the language and prominence should reflect the confidence in the evidence.

**Accountability includes correction**: a publication that holds others accountable but does not hold itself accountable — by correcting its own errors promptly — has not internalized the principle.
