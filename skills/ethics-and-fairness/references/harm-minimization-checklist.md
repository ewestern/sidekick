# Harm Minimization Checklist

A decision framework for including or excluding identifying information about individuals in coverage of government proceedings.

---

## Step 1: Classify the individual

The appropriate level of protection depends on the person's role in relation to the public proceedings being covered.

| Role | Privacy expectation | Default approach |
|---|---|---|
| Elected official (official capacity) | Low | Name, title, vote, stated position — all publishable |
| Appointed official (official capacity) | Low-medium | Name, title, recommendations, decisions — publishable |
| Government staff (official capacity) | Medium | Name and role may be published when relevant; private details protected |
| Applicant / permit seeker | Medium | Name and nature of application publishable; private financial details require judgment |
| Public commenter (testified at a public meeting) | Medium | Name and stated position publishable; they chose to speak publicly |
| Private individual as recipient of government services | High | Generally do not name without a compelling reason |
| Minor | Very high | Do not name in most circumstances |
| Crime victim | Very high | Do not name without compelling reason and consideration of harm |

---

## Step 2: Apply the public interest test

For any identifying information that might cause harm, ask:

**Is there a clear public interest justification?**
- Does naming this person serve accountability for a public action?
- Is this person's identity essential to the story, or incidental?
- Would the public interest served by naming be equally served by describing the person's role without naming them?

**Is the person a decision-maker or a recipient?**
- Decision-makers in their official capacity: naming is presumptively justified
- Recipients of services or regulatory decisions: naming requires a specific justification

**Is the potential harm proportional to the public interest?**
- High-harm scenarios: the person could face physical danger, job loss, or social ostracism
- Low-public-interest scenarios: the person is a minor figure in a larger story; naming is incidental
- If harm is disproportionate to public interest: describe role without naming

---

## Step 3: Special categories — heightened scrutiny required

**Minors**:
- Default: do not name minors in any context
- Exception: minors who are public figures in their own right (elected student board members, for example) in their public capacity only
- When minors are affected by a policy: describe the impact on the student population; do not identify specific students
- When a parent or guardian is the newsworthy party: story is about the parent, not the child

**Crime victims**:
- Default: do not name crime victims, especially in cases involving sexual assault, domestic violence, or crimes against children
- When a victim has spoken publicly: respect their choice; do not expand exposure beyond what they have consented to
- When victim identity is central to accountability (official misconduct toward a specific person): seek the victim's consent; if unavailable, strong public interest justification required

**Mental health, substance use, medical information**:
- Government records (police calls, court filings) may contain this information as incidental context
- Do not publish medical or mental health diagnoses unless directly relevant to a public accountability question and the person is a public official
- Even for public officials: their private health conditions are generally not public interest matters unless they directly affect their capacity to perform their duties

**Individuals in poverty or housing distress**:
- Recipients of housing assistance, food assistance, or other means-tested programs appear in government records
- These individuals have not sought public scrutiny by virtue of receiving assistance
- Do not name without a compelling specific reason

---

## Step 4: Practical questions before including identifying information

- [ ] Is this person's identity essential to the story, or could the story be told without it?
- [ ] What is the worst reasonably foreseeable consequence of publishing this person's name?
- [ ] Is that consequence proportional to the public interest served?
- [ ] Has the person chosen to enter the public arena (testified, applied for a permit, sought office)?
- [ ] If this person is a minor, what is the specific justification for any identification?
- [ ] If this person is a crime victim or in a vulnerable situation, has the potential for secondary harm been considered?
- [ ] If identification would cause harm, can the story be told by describing the role rather than the name?

---

## Step 5: Aggregated data vs. individual identification

When the story is about a pattern affecting many individuals, consider whether the story requires individual identification at all.

**Individual identification is needed when**:
- A specific individual's action (as a decision-maker) is the accountability subject
- A specific individual's experience (as a direct participant in a public proceeding) illustrates the pattern with their consent
- The individual's identity is itself the news (a conflict of interest involving a named person)

**Aggregate data is sufficient when**:
- The story is about a policy's effect on a population
- The story is about a systemic failure that harmed many people
- Individual cases can be described without naming the individuals
- Named individuals would face harm disproportionate to the public interest

Aggregated data ("42 families received eviction notices in the first year of the policy") can tell the story of impact without exposing any individual to additional harm.
