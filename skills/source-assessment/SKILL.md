## When this knowledge applies

When evaluating how much weight to place on information from a particular
source, determining whether a document is the authoritative record of a
decision, assessing whether a new source is reliable and recurring, or
recognizing when a source is likely incomplete or strategically framed.

## Domain knowledge

### The foundational questions for any source

Potter's questions for evaluating a source:
- How does this source know what it knows? Is it in a position to know?
- How can this information be confirmed through other sources or documents?
- How representative is the source's point of view?
- Has this source been reliable in the past?
- What is the source's motive for providing this information?

The last question is critical for government sources: agencies and officials
have incentives to present decisions favorably. A budget presentation
emphasizes progress; the underlying ledger shows transfers and adjustments.

### Trust tiers

Not all documents carry equal weight. A rough hierarchy:

**Tier 1 — Official records** (highest reliability)
- Signed meeting minutes approved by the governing body
- Adopted ordinances and resolutions (signed, numbered)
- Filed financial statements and audits
- Contracts executed by authorized parties
- Court filings and judgments

**Tier 2 — Institutional publications**
- Draft minutes (before formal approval)
- Staff reports and agenda packets (prepared by staff, not yet voted on)
- Budget proposals (what the executive requested, not what was adopted)
- Agency press releases and announcements

**Tier 3 — Media reports and summaries**
- News coverage (may contain errors; always seek the underlying record)
- Meeting summaries prepared by advocacy groups or lobbyists
- Unofficial transcripts

**Tier 4 — Anonymous or unattributed claims** (lowest standalone weight)
- Unattributed statements in documents
- Information from unnamed sources

Tier matters for framing: a claim sourced to Tier 1 can be stated as fact; a
claim from Tier 2 needs attribution; a Tier 3 or 4 claim needs corroboration
before being treated as established.

### What government bodies are legally required to publish

In the United States, local government transparency obligations typically
include (subject to state law variation):

- **Meeting agendas**: must be posted publicly before the meeting (typically
  72 hours advance notice)
- **Meeting minutes**: must be approved and made available after each meeting
- **Financial statements**: annual audited financial reports are mandatory for
  most public agencies
- **Budget documents**: proposed and adopted budgets are public records
- **Contracts**: public contracts above threshold amounts are public records
- **Ordinances and resolutions**: must be published after adoption
- **Public notices**: zoning changes, permit hearings, and environmental
  reviews require public notice

Knowledge of these obligations helps identify when expected documents are
missing — which can itself be a significant finding.

### Recognizing incomplete or strategically framed sources

Government documents are generally accurate but not necessarily complete.
Agencies frame information to support their preferred narrative. Signs that a
source may be incomplete:

- A budget document that highlights new spending but buries transfers from
  reserves
- Minutes that record a vote but omit the substance of the public comment
- A press release that announces a program without mentioning the funding cut
  that enabled it
- A staff report that presents only one option when alternatives were discussed

The absence of expected information is as significant as the presence of
unexpected information.

### What makes a source "recurring"

A recurring source is one that reliably produces documents on a predictable
schedule. Signs a source is recurring:
- Has its own page or section on a government website
- Publishes on a regular calendar (monthly, quarterly, annually)
- Has historical records going back multiple cycles
- Is referenced in other official documents as an ongoing reporting obligation

One-time documents (a commissioned study, a special audit) may be high-value
but are not recurring sources.

## Gotchas

**Officials are not omniscient.** A department head may not know what another
department decided. A council member may not know the details of a staff
report they voted on. Position authority does not equal information accuracy.

**Motive shapes what is included.** Staff reports are written to support a
recommendation; they do not neutrally present alternatives. A budget message
is written to justify the budget, not to critique it. Read with that framing
in mind.

**The absence of a document is significant.** If an agency is legally required
to file a quarterly report and no report appears, that is not a gap in coverage
— it is a potential story.

**Tier 1 documents contain errors too.** Official minutes are approved by the
governing body, but the body is approving a narrative prepared by a clerk who
may have made errors. Signed audits have been restated. Do not treat official
status as equivalent to infallibility.
