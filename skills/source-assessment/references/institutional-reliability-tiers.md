# Institutional Reliability Tiers

A detailed taxonomy of source types encountered in local government coverage, organized by reliability tier with indicators for how to weight information from each.

---

## Tier 1 — Official Records

**Defining characteristic**: Produced or formally approved by an authoritative government body; constitutes the legal record of an action.

Treat claims from Tier 1 sources as documented facts, but not as infallible. Even official records contain transcription errors, copied-forward mistakes, and version conflicts. "Official" means the body approved this as its record — it does not mean the clerk made no errors.

| Source type | Reliability notes |
|---|---|
| **Signed meeting minutes** (approved by the governing body) | The official record of what was decided. Clerk-prepared; approved at a subsequent meeting. Date of approval matters — pre-approval drafts are Tier 2. |
| **Adopted ordinances** (signed, numbered, effective-dated) | Binding law. Look for signature, effective date, and ordinance number — unsigned drafts are Tier 2. |
| **Adopted resolutions** (signed, numbered) | Formal decisions that are not law. Same caution as ordinances about version. |
| **Executed contracts** (signed by both parties, with a contract number) | The binding agreement. Staff reports describing the contract are Tier 2. |
| **Filed financial statements** (audited, signed by auditor) | Highest reliability for financial figures. Note the auditor's opinion — qualified or adverse opinions reduce weight. |
| **Court filings and judgments** | Official legal record. Note distinction between a filing (one party's claim) and a judgment (court's determination). |
| **Property records** (recorded deeds, liens, assessments) | Official county recorder record. Current as of recording date only. |
| **Secretary of State filings** (articles of incorporation, registered agents) | Official business registry. May lag actual organizational changes. |

---

## Tier 2 — Institutional Publications

**Defining characteristic**: Produced by a government body or official institution as part of standard operations, but not yet formally approved or final.

Claims from Tier 2 sources need attribution (e.g., "according to the staff report" or "in a budget proposal submitted to the council"). Do not state as established fact.

| Source type | Reliability notes |
|---|---|
| **Draft minutes** (pre-approval) | Prepared by clerk; not yet reviewed or approved by the body. May contain transcription errors. Upgrade to Tier 1 after council approval. |
| **Staff reports and agenda packets** | Prepared by staff to support a recommendation. Accurate as of publication; may be revised before the meeting. Reflects staff's perspective — see Motive below. |
| **Budget proposals** (executive's request, not yet adopted) | What the executive or manager requested; not what was actually approved. Label explicitly. |
| **Agency press releases and announcements** | Written by communications staff to reflect favorably on the agency. Accurate about facts the agency is willing to publicize; may omit or minimize unfavorable context. |
| **Annual reports** (agency-produced, not audited) | Self-reported performance data. Agencies select favorable metrics. Independent audited financials are Tier 1. |
| **Meeting audio/video** (official recording, unedited) | High reliability for what was said; lower reliability for vote tallies (relies on audio quality and identification of speakers). |

---

## Tier 3 — Media Reports and Summaries

**Defining characteristic**: Produced by parties outside the government; secondary sources that derive from or summarize primary records.

Claims from Tier 3 sources should not be treated as independently established — always seek the underlying Tier 1 or Tier 2 document. However, Tier 3 sources can be used to identify which primary documents exist and where to look.

| Source type | Reliability notes |
|---|---|
| **News coverage** | May contain errors, especially in vote tallies, dollar figures, and technical terminology. Not a substitute for the primary record. Useful for identifying that an event occurred and which documents to seek. |
| **Advocacy group summaries** | Prepared with a declared interest; may accurately describe facts while framing them selectively. Use to identify issues and documents; verify claims against Tier 1. |
| **Lobbyist-prepared summaries** | Same caution as advocacy groups; motive to present information in client's interest is explicit. |
| **Unofficial transcripts** | Quality varies. Errors in speaker identification and technical terminology are common. Audio is more reliable; official minutes are more reliable still. |
| **Social media posts by officials** | A council member's tweet about a vote is not a substitute for the minutes. Useful for tracking stated positions; not a primary record. |

---

## Tier 4 — Anonymous or Unattributed Claims

**Defining characteristic**: Source cannot be identified or does not take responsibility for the claim.

Tier 4 claims have no standalone weight. They can point toward documents to investigate, but cannot be cited or treated as establishing any fact.

| Source type | Reliability notes |
|---|---|
| **Unattributed statements in documents** | Common in staff reports ("it has been alleged that…"). The claim is only as strong as whatever supports it — locate that underlying support. |
| **Information from unnamed sources** | May be accurate and valuable for pointing toward a story. Not publishable without corroboration or attribution. |
| **Tips** | Useful investigation triggers. Treat as the beginning of verification, not its conclusion. |

---

## Motive overlay

Tier determines baseline reliability. Motive determines what to watch for within a tier.

**Government agencies**: incentive to present decisions favorably; to emphasize what went well; to omit alternatives that were rejected; to attribute problems to external constraints. Even Tier 1 records are shaped by these incentives (a budget document emphasizes new spending; the reserve draw that funded it may require closer reading).

**Applicants and developers**: incentive to present the proposed project favorably; to emphasize benefits; to minimize costs, risks, or opposition.

**Advocacy groups**: declared interest in a specific outcome; may be accurate about facts while selective in which facts they present.

**Staff**: professional, not political — but staff write reports to support their recommendations. A staff report presenting only one alternative is not evidence no alternatives existed.

**The corrective**: read for what the source has incentive to omit. The most important fact in a government document is sometimes the one that received the least emphasis.
