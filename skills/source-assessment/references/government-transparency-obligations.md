# Government Transparency Obligations

Common mandatory-disclosure documents by government body type. Subject to state law variation — this reflects typical U.S. local government requirements. The significance of these obligations: when an expected document is absent, that absence is a potential story, not just a gap in coverage.

---

## Universal obligations (most U.S. jurisdictions)

| Document | Typical requirement |
|---|---|
| **Meeting agendas** | Posted publicly before the meeting; typically 72-hour advance notice minimum (Brown Act in California; Open Meetings Act equivalents in most states) |
| **Meeting minutes** | Approved at a subsequent meeting; made publicly available after approval |
| **Annual audit** | Independent financial audit required for most agencies receiving public funds; typically due within 6 months of fiscal year end |
| **Adopted budget** | Public document; typically posted on agency website after adoption |
| **Contracts above threshold** | Most jurisdictions require public record access for contracts above a dollar threshold (thresholds vary: $5K–$25K typical for disclosure) |
| **Adopted ordinances and resolutions** | Must be published after adoption; typically posted in a designated public place or official publication |

---

## City/municipality-specific obligations

| Document | Requirement notes |
|---|---|
| **City council meeting materials** (agenda packets) | Many states require posting 72+ hours before meeting; some require same-day posting of staff reports |
| **Budget proposal** | Required before adoption hearings; public comment period required |
| **Capital improvement plan** | Often a multi-year document; updated annually in the budget process |
| **Annual financial report (CAFR/ACFR)** | Comprehensive Annual Financial Report or Annual Comprehensive Financial Report — required for most municipalities; due 6 months after fiscal year end |
| **Pension fund reports** | If the city administers a pension fund; GASB 67/68 disclosures required |
| **Public works contracts** | Above competitive bidding threshold, must go through formal solicitation process; awards are public record |
| **Land use and zoning decisions** | Public hearings required; findings of fact must be in writing for discretionary approvals |

---

## School district-specific obligations

| Document | Requirement notes |
|---|---|
| **Board meeting agendas and minutes** | Same open meetings requirements as other public bodies |
| **Annual budget** | Adopted publicly; typically with a 45-day public comment period |
| **Financial audit** | Required annually; state education agency typically sets the reporting deadline |
| **LCAP / accountability plans** | In California and equivalent states: Local Control and Accountability Plan, updated annually with public input |
| **Enrollment and demographic data** | Reported annually to state; public record |
| **Staff salary schedules** | Public record in most states |
| **Collective bargaining agreements** | Public record once ratified |

---

## Special district obligations

Special districts (water, fire, transit, sanitation, mosquito abatement, etc.) vary significantly by state and enabling statute. At minimum, most are subject to:

- Open meetings requirements
- Annual audit or financial report
- Budget adoption process with public notice
- Contract award disclosures above threshold

**Gotcha**: Special districts often have weaker transparency infrastructure than cities or counties — fewer staff, less consistent website maintenance, and less media scrutiny. Missing documents from a special district may reflect capacity constraints rather than intentional concealment — but the obligation still exists and the absence matters.

## County/regional body obligations

| Document | Requirement notes |
|---|---|
| **Board of supervisors meeting materials** | Same open meetings requirements |
| **Property tax assessment rolls** | Public record; typically searchable via assessor's website |
| **Campaign finance filings** | State and local candidates must file; deadlines and thresholds vary by state |
| **Lobbying disclosure** | Many jurisdictions require registration and disclosure of contacts with elected officials |
| **Grant records** | Federal grant awards (CFDA/FAIN numbers) are publicly disclosed via USASpending.gov; state grants vary |

---

## Federal pass-through transparency

For local governments receiving federal funds:

| Source | Public access |
|---|---|
| **Federal contracts and grants** | USASpending.gov — searchable by recipient, program, award amount |
| **Community Development Block Grants** | HUD CPD Maps; detailed expenditure data available |
| **FEMA disaster grants** | Publicly searchable after declared disasters |
| **Education formula grants (Title I, IDEA)** | State education agencies report allocations; federal EDGAR database |

## The absent document

When an expected document is missing:

1. **Is it actually missing?** Check: different section of the website, different naming convention, different agency (county vs. city vs. district)
2. **Is it late?** Audit due 6 months after fiscal year end; if it's month 8, the absence is significant
3. **Was there a legal exemption claimed?** Some deliberations are legitimately closed; determine if the closed-session justification is valid
4. **Is it a pattern?** A single missing quarterly report may be an oversight; three consecutive missing reports is a compliance failure

The absence of a required document is itself a newsworthy fact — not a gap in coverage to be filled, but a potential story about an agency's compliance with its transparency obligations.
