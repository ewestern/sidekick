# Budget Analysis Guide

How to read a municipal budget document from scratch — structure, key sections, what to look for, and common traps.

---

## Step 1: Identify what type of budget document you have

Before analyzing, establish what the document is:

| Document type | What it contains | Key distinction |
|---|---|---|
| **Budget proposal** | Executive's recommended spending plan | Not yet approved; subject to change |
| **Adopted budget** | Council-approved appropriations | Legal authority to spend; final for that year |
| **Mid-year budget adjustment** | Amendments to the adopted budget | Changes approved after adoption |
| **CAFR / ACFR** | Comprehensive annual financial report | Actual expenditures, not budgeted — audited |
| **Quarterly financial report** | Mid-year actual vs. budget tracking | Shows where spending is ahead/behind plan |

**The most significant discrepancies occur between the proposal and the adopted budget** (what the council changed) and **between the adopted budget and actuals** (what actually happened).

---

## Step 2: Read the executive summary / budget message

The budget message is written by the city manager or mayor to present the administration's priorities. It is persuasive, not neutral. Read it for:

- **Headline numbers**: total revenues, total expenditures, any reserve use
- **Stated priorities**: what programs are emphasized; what constraints are cited
- **What is not mentioned**: a budget message that does not mention a pension liability, a deferred maintenance backlog, or service reductions may be omitting material facts

Cross-check every positive claim in the budget message against the actual numbers in the document.

---

## Step 3: Check the structural balance

The most important single question about any operating budget: do recurring revenues cover recurring expenditures?

Structural imbalance exists when:
- A budget uses one-time revenues (asset sales, reserve draws, federal stimulus) to cover ongoing expenditures
- A budget defers obligations (maintenance, pension contributions, contract renewals) that will compound later
- A budget relies on revenue projections that are optimistic relative to recent actuals

**Where to find it**:
- Fund summary table: compare total revenues vs. total expenditures. Any gap is covered by either reserves (draw-down) or borrowing.
- Reserve fund schedule: compare opening and closing reserve balance. A declining reserve is being spent down.
- "One-time revenue" labels: look for line items labeled "one-time" or "non-recurring" on the revenue side.

---

## Step 4: Review department-by-department year-over-year changes

Compare each department's appropriation against the prior year's adopted budget. Note:

| Change | Signal |
|---|---|
| > 10% increase | Significant growth — why? New program? Increased costs? |
| > 10% decrease | Significant cut — services affected? Positions eliminated? |
| Position count change | Every FTE added or removed has multi-year personnel cost implications |
| Line item shifted from operating to capital (or vice versa) | May be reclassification; may be a structural change |
| New line item | New program, grant, or obligation |
| Eliminated line item | Program ended or consolidated |

**Personnel is the largest driver** in most operating budgets. A "flat" budget that absorbs salary and benefit cost increases through cuts elsewhere may represent significant service reductions even if the headline number did not change.

---

## Step 5: Check the capital improvement plan (CIP)

The CIP is a multi-year plan for major capital expenditures. It is not the operating budget. Look for:

- **Projects added from prior CIP**: new commitments
- **Projects removed or deferred**: what capital investment is being postponed?
- **Funding sources**: is the CIP funded by bonds (future debt), grants (federal/state funds), developer fees (tied to development activity), or general fund transfers (competition with operating budget)?
- **Project cost changes**: did a project's cost increase significantly between the prior CIP and the current one? By how much? Is there a stated reason?

**Deferred maintenance** is a red flag: if the CIP shows infrastructure projects (roads, buildings, utilities) being deferred year after year, the agency is building up a maintenance backlog that will be more expensive to address later.

---

## Step 6: Check the debt section

Find the debt service schedule (may be in the CIP or in a separate section). Look for:

- **Total outstanding debt** (all bonds and obligations)
- **Annual debt service** (principal + interest payments due this year)
- **Debt service as % of total revenues**: above 15% is a warning sign; above 20% indicates significant financial constraint
- **New debt issuance planned**: any bonds proposed for approval? What are they funding?
- **Debt maturity profile**: when does existing debt come due? A debt "cliff" creates future budget pressure.

---

## Step 7: Check pension and OPEB disclosures

Pensions (retirement benefits) and OPEB (other post-employment benefits, typically retiree health insurance) are often the largest off-balance-sheet liabilities in local government.

**Where to find it**:
- Notes to financial statements in the CAFR/ACFR
- Actuarial valuation reports (may be separate documents)
- Budget section on required contributions

**Key metrics**:
- **Unfunded actuarial liability (UAL)**: the difference between what the pension plan owes and what it has. Growing UAL means the problem is getting worse.
- **Funded ratio**: plan assets as a percent of total liabilities. Below 70% is concerning; below 60% is a significant fiscal stress indicator.
- **Annual required contribution (ARC)**: what actuaries say the agency should contribute. If the agency is contributing less than the ARC, the liability is growing.

## Common red flags

| Observation | What it may indicate |
|---|---|
| Operating reserve draw to balance budget, third year in a row | Structural deficit; the agency is spending more than it earns on a recurring basis |
| Capital projects deferred repeatedly | Maintenance backlog accumulating |
| Pension funded ratio below 70% | Significant unfunded obligation constraining future budgets |
| "Reductions in force" in the personnel section | Service-level cuts; positions eliminated |
| Significant mid-year budget amendments | Actual conditions diverged significantly from budget assumptions |
| Revenue projections above recent actuals for the same revenue source | Optimistic projections that may not materialize |
| New debt with vague or broad scope ("economic development") | Limited accountability for how bond funds are used |
