# Follow the Money Guide

A structured methodology for tracing financial flows from public expenditure decisions to ultimate beneficiaries, based on the Data Journalism Handbook cross-border investigation framework.

---

## The core chain

Every public expenditure follows a chain from authorization to impact:

```
Public decision → Appropriation → Contract/Grant award → Payment → Recipient → Beneficial owner
```

An investigation "follows the money" by tracing each link in this chain and identifying who benefits at the end, then working backward to see who made the decisions and whether relationships exist between the decision-makers and beneficiaries.

---

## Step 1: Identify the expenditure

Start with a public financial commitment:
- A contract award (amount, recipient, purpose)
- A budget appropriation (line item, amount, authorized use)
- A grant award (amount, recipient, program)
- A bond issuance (amount, purpose, underwriter)

**What to record at this step**:
- The authorizing document (contract number, resolution number, budget line)
- The stated amount and term
- The stated purpose (scope of work, program description)
- Whether the award was competitive or non-competitive (sole-source, no-bid)
- Who voted to approve it, and any recusals

---

## Step 2: Identify the immediate recipient

The immediate recipient is the legal entity named in the contract or grant award.

**Common recipient structures**:
- Corporation (Inc., Corp.)
- Limited liability company (LLC, PLLC)
- Partnership (LP, LLP)
- Nonprofit corporation (typically 501(c)(3) or (c)(6))
- Government agency (another public entity as recipient)

**Research steps**:
- Secretary of State business registry: registered name, formation date, registered agent
- County recorder: if the entity owns property, it may appear in deed records
- IRS: for nonprofits, Form 990 is public — shows revenue, expenses, board members, compensation
- OpenCorporates or similar aggregators: for corporate relationship mapping

**Key question**: is the legal entity the same as the entity described in the contract? A contract awarded to "ABC Consulting" may be executed with "ABC Consulting LLC" which is a separate legal entity from "ABC Consulting Inc."

---

## Step 3: Identify the beneficial owner

The beneficial owner is the natural person (or persons) who ultimately controls or benefits from the entity.

**Where to find beneficial ownership information**:
- Secretary of State filings: managers, officers, directors (disclosure requirements vary by state and entity type)
- IRS Form 990: lists officers, directors, key employees, and their compensation for nonprofits
- UCC filings: secured creditors sometimes reveal ownership structures
- Court records: litigation involving the entity may disclose ownership
- Campaign finance filings: entity officers may appear as political contributors
- Professional licensing: engineers, lawyers, accountants, architects must register personally

**Common obfuscation structures**:
- Holding companies: Entity A is owned by Entity B, which is owned by Entity C
- Shell LLCs with "member managed" structures that hide individual names
- Nominee shareholders or directors
- Cross-state structures (a Nevada LLC holding California contracts)

**The beneficial ownership gap**: the U.S. lacks a comprehensive beneficial ownership registry. Investigation often requires assembling information from multiple sources.

---

## Step 4: Identify the decision-makers

Who authorized the expenditure? This includes:
- Who voted on the approving resolution or contract award
- Who recommended the award (staff, department director, procurement office)
- Who set the evaluation criteria
- Who had discretion in a sole-source or emergency award
- Who had authority to waive competitive bidding requirements

**Recusals**: note any recusals by voting members — a recusal indicates a declared conflict of interest. Track whether the recused member's stated reason (typically "financial interest") connects to the beneficial ownership chain.

---

## Step 5: Map the connections

Build a relationship map between the beneficial owners (Step 3) and the decision-makers (Step 4). Research:

**Financial connections**:
- Campaign contributions: did the beneficial owner, their family members, or their business entities contribute to the campaigns of the approving officials?
- Personal financial disclosure: most elected officials and senior appointed officials are required to file annual financial disclosure. These may reveal investments, ownership interests, or financial relationships.
- Prior contracts: did the beneficial owner receive prior contracts from the same agency?

**Personal connections**:
- Employment history: did the beneficial owner previously work for the agency, or did agency staff previously work for the beneficiary?
- Board membership: does the beneficial owner sit on any board or commission that gives them influence over the agency?
- Professional associations: are they members of the same professional organizations?

**Political connections**:
- Endorsements: did the beneficial owner or their organization endorse the approving official in a prior election?
- Fundraising: did they host a fundraiser or bundle contributions?
- Lobbying: did they register as a lobbyist or hire one who contacted the agency about the contract?

---

## Step 6: Assess and document

For each connection identified, assess:

**Strength of connection**:
- Strong: documented financial relationship, verifiable in public records
- Moderate: documented professional or political relationship
- Weak: shared social network, common membership, proximity

**Timing**:
- Did the connection predate the award decision? (Potentially relevant)
- Did the connection develop after the award? (Less likely to be causal)

**Pattern**:
- Is this a single instance or a repeating pattern?
- Does the same beneficial owner receive awards from multiple agencies where connected officials serve?

**The publication standard**: a documented connection is a fact that can be reported. An inference about causation (the connection influenced the award) requires either a pattern of evidence that makes the inference compelling, or a source who confirms it on the record.

---

## Step 7: Check the deliverable

Complete the chain by confirming what was actually delivered:

- Was the scope of work completed?
- Were payments made on schedule, or were there disputes?
- Are there amendments to the contract? (Each amendment is its own expenditure decision)
- What was the final total cost vs. the original award?
- Was the deliverable publicly disclosed (a report, a completed project, a service)?

**Deliverable gaps**: a contract that produced no public deliverable, or a deliverable that cannot be confirmed, is itself a finding.

---

## Documentation checklist

For any follow-the-money investigation, maintain:

- [ ] Original contract/award document
- [ ] Payment records (invoices paid, if obtainable)
- [ ] Entity registration documents
- [ ] Beneficial ownership documentation
- [ ] Decision-maker roster (who voted, who recommended)
- [ ] Campaign finance records for decision-makers
- [ ] Personal financial disclosure for decision-makers
- [ ] Prior contracts with same recipient
- [ ] Timeline of relationships vs. timeline of awards
- [ ] Deliverables documentation (final reports, completed work)
