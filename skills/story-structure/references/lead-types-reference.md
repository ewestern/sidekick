# Lead Types Reference

Hard lead, soft lead, and anecdotal lead examples from government reporting, with annotations.

---

## Hard leads (summary leads)

The hard lead states the key fact directly in the first sentence. It answers the most important news value immediately.

**Vote outcome lead**:
> "Springfield's city council voted 5-2 Tuesday to raise water rates 12 percent, adding about $8 a month to the average household bill starting in January."

Annotations:
- Vote tally: included (5-2 signals a contested vote)
- Dollar impact: translated to what it means for a household
- Effective date: included — readers know when this affects them
- "Average household" — contextualizes the abstract percentage

**Contract award lead**:
> "The Springfield School District awarded a $4.8 million technology contract Tuesday to a company whose founder made campaign contributions to three of the board members who voted to approve it."

Annotations:
- Leads with the newsworthy complication, not the routine fact (not "The board approved a contract")
- Specific dollar amount in the lead
- The conflict connection is in the lead — it is the news angle

**Policy change lead**:
> "Springfield will no longer send police officers to mental health calls under a plan the city council unanimously approved Wednesday, making it one of the first cities in the state to shift such calls to civilian responders."

Annotations:
- Active construction: "Springfield will no longer" — concrete and specific
- The significance (first in state) is noted but not over-claimed
- The mechanism (civilian responders) is briefly explained

**Disclosure lead**:
> "Springfield's parks department has been operating for three years without a required financial audit, city records show — a gap that officials acknowledged for the first time Tuesday."

Annotations:
- The finding is the lead, not the meeting at which it was discussed
- "City records show" — source attribution without over-cluttering the lead
- "Officials acknowledged for the first time Tuesday" — adds the newsworthiness of the disclosure moment

## Soft leads (tease leads)

The soft lead withholds the central fact briefly to create engagement. It must be paired with a hard nut graf — the reader cannot wait more than two paragraphs for the news.

**Question lead** (use sparingly):
> "What does a public pool manager make in Springfield? Until recently, even the city didn't know."

Annotation: Acceptable only if the answer is immediately compelling and the nut graf follows instantly. Overused; often feels like a gimmick.

**Contrast lead**:
> "A year ago, Lisa Chen's job was helping families navigate city housing assistance. Today, she is one of them."

Annotation: Works when the contrast genuinely encapsulates the story's theme. Must be followed immediately by the nut graf explaining the policy change that created this situation.

**Scene lead** (gateway to narrative structure):
> "The council chamber was still half-empty at 9 p.m. Tuesday when Rodriguez finally took the microphone — 45 minutes after the scheduled start of the hearing on the proposed shelter."

Annotation: Opens with scene rather than summary; appropriate only for narrative/hourglass structure. The reader must quickly understand why this moment matters.

## Anecdotal leads (narrative structure)

The anecdotal lead opens with a specific person or event before widening to the issue. Always requires a nut graf.

**Person-in-affected-situation lead**:
> "James Morales has coached youth baseball at Garfield Park for eleven years. He knows every family in the league, every kid who has cycled through his team. He does not know yet that the city plans to eliminate his program.
>
> The Springfield parks department's proposed budget, released Tuesday, would cut 40 percent of its youth programming to close a $1.2 million funding gap — eliminating programs that serve an estimated 3,000 children."

Annotations:
- Opening three sentences: specific, concrete, human
- The reveal ("He does not know yet") creates engagement without being manipulative — the budget is public record
- Nut graf (fourth paragraph) delivers the scope immediately
- "An estimated 3,000 children" — context that makes the impact concrete

**Before/after anecdote lead**:
> "When the Springfield City Council approved a new zoning code in 2021, Elena Reyes thought it meant her neighborhood was finally protected from the kind of dense development that had transformed the district to the east.
>
> She was wrong. Three years later, a 150-unit apartment complex is under construction two blocks from her house — built under an exemption in the same code that was supposed to protect her neighborhood."

Annotations:
- Timeline built into the lead: 2021 → now
- A specific person whose experience illustrates the gap between policy intent and outcome
- The specificity ("150-unit apartment complex") makes it concrete, not abstract

## Common lead failures

**The meeting lead** (what not to do):
> ~~"The Springfield City Council held its regular meeting Tuesday night at City Hall."~~
Why it fails: no reader needs to know the council met. Lead with what was decided.

**The process lead**:
> ~~"After months of study and public comment, the planning commission voted Tuesday on a proposal that would change the city's zoning code."~~
Why it fails: buries the outcome. What did they vote? Which direction?

**The everything lead**:
> ~~"The Springfield City Council voted Tuesday to approve a $3.2 million contract with ABC Services for pool management, lay off 22 employees, and direct the parks director to prepare a report on the long-term fiscal implications."~~
Why it fails: three separate facts competing for priority. Pick the most newsworthy one.

**The attribution-first lead**:
> ~~"According to a staff report released Tuesday by the city manager's office, the Springfield parks department has experienced a significant shortfall in revenues compared to projections made at the beginning of the fiscal year."~~
Why it fails: buries the lead in attribution and passive framing. "The parks department is running $800,000 short of its revenue targets, city records show."
