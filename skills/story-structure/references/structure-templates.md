# Structure Templates

Annotated templates for each story structure with guidance for local government stories.

---

## Inverted pyramid

**Best for**: breaking government news, vote outcomes, contract awards, official announcements

```
[LEAD]
One or two sentences. The single most important fact.
Who did what, to whom, when, with what consequence.
Example: "The Springfield City Council voted 4-3 Tuesday to approve a $3.2 million
contract for a private company to manage the city's three public pools, over the
objections of the parks employees' union."

[SECOND PARAGRAPH — The "Why It Matters"]
Immediate significance or consequence. Answers: so what?
Example: "The decision, which takes effect July 1, means the city will lay off
22 full-time parks employees, most of whom have worked for the city for more
than a decade."

[THE DISSENT / CONFLICT]
The opposing view, briefly stated.
Example: "Council Member James Torres, who voted against the contract, said the
city had not adequately studied the long-term costs of privatization."

[SUPPORTING DETAIL]
Vote tally specifics, contract details, the winning argument.

[BACKGROUND / CONTEXT]
Prior history, why this came up, relevant prior decisions.

[DISTANT CONTEXT / ADDITIONAL STAKEHOLDER VOICES]
Broader trends, expert perspective, secondary affected parties.

[BOILERPLATE / PROCEDURAL DETAIL]
When the decision takes effect; what happens next procedurally.
```

**Common mistakes**:
- Leading with "The Springfield City Council met Tuesday…" — bury the meeting, lead with the decision
- Burying the dollar amount in paragraph four — if it's newsworthy, it goes near the top
- Not stating the vote tally — 4-3 is news; 7-0 is context

## Hourglass

**Best for**: contested proceedings where the drama of the meeting is itself the story

```
[NEWS SUMMARY — 2-4 paragraphs, inverted pyramid style]
What was decided; who was affected; the key fact.

[THE TURN]
A transitional phrase that signals the shift to narrative.
"Here's what happened." / "The vote came after nearly three hours of testimony."

[CHRONOLOGICAL NARRATIVE]
The story told in time order:
- Who appeared at the public hearing
- What they said
- How the deliberation developed
- The key moment where the outcome became clear
- The vote

[CODA / PRESENT MOMENT]
Return to the present: what happens next, reaction from the parties.
```

**When it works**: a public hearing where a developer's project was approved over organized neighborhood opposition; an emergency council session where a controversial official was removed; a school board meeting where a budget decision produced a walkout.

**When it doesn't**: when the meeting was uneventful and the outcome is the only news.

## Narrative / diamond

**Best for**: features, trend stories, human-interest angles on policy decisions

```
[ANECDOTE / SCENE — 2-4 paragraphs]
A specific person in a specific moment that illustrates the larger issue.
Her name, her situation, the concrete detail. Not "many residents are affected."
"Maria Gutierrez stood in the parking lot of the Roosevelt Park pool on a Tuesday
afternoon, watching her two children splash in water she worried they would soon
have nowhere else to swim."

[NUT GRAF]
The bridge from specific to general. What is this story about?
Why does it matter now? Who else is affected?
"Gutierrez is one of thousands of Springfield families who use the city's three
public pools — facilities that a private management company will take over July 1,
under a council vote last week that sparked a heated public debate about the value
of public services."

[BODY]
Data and context that establish the scope of the issue.
Opposing views; expert perspective; relevant history.
Multiple voices representing different affected stakeholders.

[IMPLICATIONS / WHAT HAPPENS NEXT]
Where the issue stands; what is still unresolved; what the affected
parties will do.

[KICKER — return to the opening person or scene]
Return to Maria, or to the pool, or to the concrete image from the opening.
Has something changed? What remains unresolved for her?
"Gutierrez said she planned to come back to the pool, whoever was running it.
But she had not told her children yet about the layoffs."
```

**The anecdote selection**: the opening person or scene must genuinely represent the issue. A cherry-picked extreme case ("the one family most devastated") misleads readers about scope. Choose someone whose situation is illustrative, not exceptional.

## Chronological (explainer)

**Best for**: complex processes, "how did we get here" explainers, investigative timelines

```
[BRIEF SUMMARY — 1-2 paragraphs]
Where we are now; why this timeline matters.

[TIMELINE SECTION]
Dated entries in chronological order.
Each entry: what happened, why it matters to the overall story.

[PRESENT STATUS]
Where things stand today.

[WHAT COMES NEXT]
Scheduled decisions, pending matters, unresolved questions.
```

**When to use**: the sequence of events is genuinely difficult to understand without step-by-step narration. An investigation where decisions made years apart are connected. A disputed process where the order of events is contested.

**What to avoid**: using chronological structure as a substitute for analysis. A timeline of events is not a story unless the events are connected by a through-line that the structure reveals.
