## When this knowledge applies

When evaluating whether a story draft is well-organized, when determining
the most effective structure for a particular story type, or when assessing
whether a lead, nut graf, or ending is doing its job.

## Domain knowledge

### The inverted pyramid

The oldest and most common news structure. Most important information at the
top; detail and background descend in order of decreasing importance. The
structure allows editors to cut from the bottom without losing essential facts.

**When to use**: breaking news, short briefs, straightforward government
actions where the outcome is the news. A council vote, a contract award, a
permit decision. The reader should know what happened by the end of the first
paragraph.

**The lead** (first paragraph): answers who, what, when, where — and ideally
why and how, though these may require more space. Should be a single sentence
or two short sentences. No more than 35 words in most cases.

**The body**: elaborates on the lead in descending order of importance. Direct
quotes, background, context, and detail each occupy progressively less prime
real estate.

**The weakness**: stories that read like grocery lists. Every paragraph is
equally important. Readers who skip around miss nothing — but they also feel
nothing.

### The hourglass

Combines the inverted pyramid with a narrative section. Opens with hard news
(like the pyramid), then transitions with a "turn" into a chronological
telling of events that leads to the outcome already stated in the opening.

**Structure**:
1. News summary (2-4 paragraphs: the pyramid's top)
2. Turn phrase ("Here's how it happened:" or similar)
3. Chronological narrative
4. Conclusion that returns to the present

**When to use**: stories where the process of how something happened is itself
the news — a contested vote, a confrontational public hearing, a sequence of
events leading to an outcome. The reader knows the ending from the top; the
narrative section shows why it matters.

### The narrative / diamond structure

Begins with a specific scene or person (the anecdote lead), widens to the
broader issue (the nut graf), tells the story with context and detail, then
returns to the opening scene or person at the end.

**Structure**:
1. Anecdote or scene (specific, vivid, compelling)
2. Nut graf: "this is what this story is about; here is why it matters"
3. Body: data, context, opposing views, expert voices
4. Kicker: return to the opening scene or person; what has changed or what
   remains unresolved

**When to use**: feature stories, trend pieces, human-interest angles on
government decisions. A story about a family affected by a housing policy, a
business owner navigating a new regulation, a school impacted by a budget cut.
The specific example makes the abstract policy concrete.

**The weakness**: requires a strong anecdote that genuinely represents the
larger issue. An anecdote that is atypical or cherry-picked misleads readers
about the scope of the problem.

### Chronological

Events told in time order. Rarely the best structure for news; useful for
explanatory pieces where the sequence of events is genuinely difficult to
understand without step-by-step narration.

**When to use**: explainers ("how did we get here?"), investigative narratives
where the timeline is the finding, or procedural guides. Rare in daily coverage.

### The lead

The first paragraph is the single most important part of any news story.
Readers who don't continue past the lead still receive the most important fact.

**Hard lead (summary lead)**: states the key fact directly. "The Springfield
City Council approved a $2.4 million contract Tuesday for a new police records
system, the largest technology purchase in the city's history."

**Soft lead (anecdotal lead)**: opens with a specific scene or person before
widening to the news. Used with the narrative structure; requires a nut graf
to deliver the news.

**What makes a strong hard lead**:
- One central fact (not three)
- Specific (dollar amount, vote tally, named party — not vague)
- Active voice
- No jargon
- Answers the most important news value (the most newsworthy fact is the lead)

**What makes a weak lead**:
- Burying the news behind context or background
- "The city council met Tuesday night…" (who cares that they met?)
- Multiple facts competing for priority
- Passive voice obscuring who did what

### The nut graf

In feature and narrative stories, the nut graf is the paragraph that tells
the reader what the story is really about and why it matters. It follows the
opening anecdote and bridges from the specific to the general.

Downing: Headline + Subhead = Lead. The nut graf expands on the headline
premise: "This is the story because…"

A strong nut graf answers: what is the broader issue this anecdote represents?
Why does it matter now? Who else is affected?

### Endings

**The kicker**: returns to the opening scene or person; provides closure.
The best kickers echo the beginning without repeating it, and leave the
reader with a final image or thought that resonates.

**The implications ending**: states what happens next, who is watching, what
the decision means going forward. Used in hard news when the consequences are
still unfolding.

**The quote ending**: a strong final quote from a source that captures the
emotional or political significance of the story. The quote must earn its
place — it should say something the reporter could not say directly, with
authenticity or passion.

**What not to do**: ending on a mundane fact, a bureaucratic summary, or a
quote that merely restates what was already said. Endings should feel
deliberate, not like the writer ran out of content.

## Gotchas

**Structure follows purpose.** Choose the structure that serves the story's
central question. An inverted pyramid for a feature feels cold; a narrative
structure for a breaking council vote buries the news.

**The lead must earn every word.** Every word in a lead that does not carry
essential information is taking space from something that does. "The Springfield
City Council, at its regularly scheduled meeting Tuesday evening, voted…" — cut
everything before "voted."

**Nut grafs can be too broad.** "This is part of a national trend in housing
policy" is technically a nut graf but it does not tell the reader what *this*
story is specifically about. The nut graf must be specific enough to set up
the body that follows.
