## When this knowledge applies

When a needed document may exist but is not available through routine public
channels, when assessing whether an agency is complying with its transparency
obligations, when identifying what records can be obtained through a formal
request, or when reasoning about the significance of a document's absence.

## Domain knowledge

### The legal framework

**Federal Freedom of Information Act (FOIA)**: applies to federal executive
branch agencies. Requests made to federal agencies for records they hold.
Processing times vary from weeks to years; agencies may charge for search,
duplication, and review. Nine statutory exemptions allow agencies to withhold
specific categories of information.

**State public records laws**: all 50 U.S. states have equivalents to FOIA.
Names vary: California Public Records Act (CPRA), Texas Public Information
Act, Florida Government-in-the-Sunshine Law, New York Freedom of Information
Law (FOIL). These govern state agencies and local government — city councils,
school boards, special districts.

**State laws matter more for local government coverage**: a city council's
records are subject to the state public records law, not federal FOIA. Know
the applicable state law for the jurisdiction being covered.

**The Data Journalism Handbook ("Wobbing Works")**: "wobbing" (from WOB,
the Swedish acronym for freedom of information) — the systematic use of
public records requests to obtain government data — is a core technique of
data journalism. The handbook documents case after case where systematic
FOIA/public records requests revealed what routine press releases concealed.

### What is public without a request (automatically disclosed)

Most jurisdictions require automatic public disclosure (no request needed)
for:
- Meeting agendas (posted in advance)
- Adopted meeting minutes (posted after approval)
- Adopted budgets and budget proposals
- Adopted ordinances and resolutions
- Annual financial audits and reports (CAFR/ACFR)
- Contracts above disclosure thresholds
- Salary schedules for public employees
- Campaign finance filings

These should be findable on the agency's website. Absence from the website
does not mean they don't exist — they may be obtainable on request without
formal FOIA process.

### What requires a formal request

Records that exist but are not routinely published:
- Email correspondence among officials about a specific matter
- Internal communications (texts, memos, voicemails)
- Contracts below the automatic disclosure threshold
- Invoices and payment records
- Personnel files (with significant exemptions for private personnel matters)
- Bids and proposals received in procurement
- Inspection reports and enforcement actions
- Prior drafts of public documents
- Calendars and scheduling records of officials

### Common exemptions and how to work around them

**Deliberative process / pre-decisional**: drafts, working papers, internal
analysis prepared before a decision was made. This exemption is frequently
over-applied. Records that were used in making a decision (as opposed to
being merely preparatory) may not be exempt.

*Work-around*: request "all records, including final and non-draft documents,
used in the preparation of [decision]" — final records are not exempt even
if labeled as drafts.

**Personnel records**: individual employee information is often exempt to
protect personal privacy. But aggregate salary data, total compensation for
senior officials, and records of disciplinary actions resulting in termination
or demotion are often disclosable.

*Work-around*: request aggregate data rather than individual records; request
specifically for senior officials who have reduced privacy expectations.

**Pending litigation / attorney-client privilege**: communications with the
city attorney on a pending matter. Broad, but does not protect all agency
legal communications — only those that are specifically privileged legal advice.

*Work-around*: request records that do not constitute legal advice; request
documents that predate the litigation.

**Law enforcement investigative records**: active criminal investigations
may be exempt; closed investigations often are not.

**Trade secrets / proprietary information**: contract bids may include trade
secret designations. Agencies sometimes accept these designations without
scrutiny. The designation must be genuine to be valid.

*Work-around*: request the agency's determination of what is actually trade
secret; challenge over-broad designations.

### Crafting an effective request

**Be specific**: a broad request ("all records about contracts") will produce
a slow, expensive, and incomplete response. A specific request ("all records
related to Contract No. 2026-112 with ABC Consulting, including the solicitation,
bids received, evaluation documents, and payment records") is more likely to
produce what is needed.

**Name the specific document if you know it exists**: "the staff report
prepared for the March 11, 2026 city council meeting regarding Agenda Item 7B."

**Set a date range**: unbounded requests invite delay; a reasonable date range
focuses the search.

**Request in an electronic format**: most state laws allow requesters to
specify format. Requesting an electronic spreadsheet rather than paper enables
analysis.

**Note your media status**: most states have fee waivers or reductions for
media requesters. Some states have expedited processing for media.

### Government data portals (no request needed)

Before filing a request, check whether the data already exists in a
structured form:

| Source | What it contains |
|---|---|
| USASpending.gov | Federal contracts, grants, loans by recipient and agency |
| SAM.gov | Federal contractor registrations and award data |
| State transparency portals | Vary by state; often include vendor payments, contracts |
| OpenCheckbook / similar | Some cities post all expenditures online |
| City/county open data portals | Often include permits, business licenses, code enforcement |
| SEC EDGAR | Financial disclosures for public companies (relevant if a public company is a vendor) |
| IRS EINS search | Basic nonprofit data; Form 990s available via ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer |
| County assessor / recorder | Property ownership, deed transfers, recorded liens |
| Secretary of State | Business registry, corporate filings, registered agents |

### The absence of a response as a story

An agency's failure to respond to a public records request within the legally
required time frame is itself a potential story. Most state laws require
acknowledgment within 3-10 days and production within a reasonable time
(often 10-30 days, with extensions). A pattern of non-response or excessive
delay may indicate:
- Resource constraints (high volume, understaffed records office)
- Deliberate obstruction
- Records management failures

Document every step of a request: the date submitted, any acknowledgment,
any extension claimed, the date of production, and any withholding.

## Gotchas

**"Public record" does not always mean "publicly posted."** A document may
be a public record that is available on request but not published. The city
keeps all contracts; it may only post contracts above a certain threshold
online. The rest are obtainable by request.

**State laws vary significantly.** California's CPRA is among the most
expansive; other states have much more limited requirements. A practice
permissible in one jurisdiction may be a violation in another. Know the
law for the specific jurisdiction.

**Exemptions must be asserted specifically.** An agency cannot simply decline
a request without identifying the specific exemption that applies. If no
exemption is identified, the refusal may be legally invalid.

**Electronic records include metadata.** A request for email correspondence
should specify that it includes metadata (sent/received times, sender/recipient
addresses) — agencies sometimes produce PDF printouts that strip metadata.
