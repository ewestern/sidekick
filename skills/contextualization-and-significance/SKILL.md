## When this knowledge applies

When a beat item appears potentially significant but the reason for its
importance is not yet fully established. Use this when writing a brief,
framing a flag, or deciding whether missing context warrants a follow-up
research assignment.

## Domain knowledge

### The core test

Every candidate lead should survive these questions:

- What happened?
- Why does it matter now?
- Compared to what?
- Is this routine, anomalous, contested, or high-impact?
- Who is affected?
- What remains unknown?

If the story cannot answer "compared to what?" or "why now?" it may still be
newsworthy, but the significance claim must remain narrow until more context
is gathered.

### Context sources in order

Try to establish context in this order:

1. Current-scope processed artifacts
2. Broader historical artifacts on the same beat/geo
3. Lineage-related source material tied to the event
4. External follow-up through research assignment

Do not jump to external retrieval if the artifact store already contains enough
comparison or prior history to support the claim.

### What counts as enough context

**For contract and spending stories**
- Prior contract values or amendments
- Comparable contracts for the same service
- Share of the relevant departmental budget
- Procurement posture: routine renewal, emergency, sole-source, or no-bid
- Vendor history with the same agency

**For votes and proceedings**
- Whether the item was routine or pulled from consent
- Whether the vote was unanimous, split, delayed, or reconsidered
- Whether the action changed policy, spending, or services in the real world

**For anomaly claims**
- A baseline period or comparable case
- A plausible explanation grounded in records
- Clear separation between documented fact and unresolved suspicion

### When to create a research assignment

Create a research follow-up when:

- The event is significant and additional context could materially sharpen or validate the story
- A likely primary or supporting document is missing
- The best available context is only secondary-source framing
- The story points toward vendor history, procurement history, or prior-period comparisons not present in artifacts

Do not create a follow-up just because more detail could exist somewhere. The
missing context should be actionable and likely to improve editorial judgment.

### Relationship to significance

Need for research is orthogonal to significance.

- If the event is significant on its own, write the brief now.
- If additional context would improve the framing, create a research assignment.
- If the event is not significant, do not inflate it just because the missing
  context sounds interesting.

### Versioning follow-up

If later research materially improves a published brief:

- Write a new brief version
- Supersede the earlier brief
- Let editorial consumers default to the newest version

Do not rewrite history in place; keep the earlier version available for traceability.

## Gotchas

**Raw increases are not self-explanatory.** "Spending increased" is incomplete
without baseline, proportion, or prior history.

**Context should narrow claims, not decorate them.** If the only supported fact
is that a contract amount rose, do not imply conflict, scandal, or crisis
without records supporting that leap.

**Missing context is not a license to withhold every brief.** Significant events
still deserve timely coverage, even when follow-up reporting remains necessary.
