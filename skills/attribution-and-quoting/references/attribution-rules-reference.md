# Attribution Rules Reference

When to attribute, when not to, and how to attribute accurately — with local government examples.

---

## No attribution needed

These can be stated as facts, without "according to" or similar:

**Actions taken in official proceedings**
- "The council voted 4-3 to approve the contract." (from the minutes)
- "The ordinance takes effect June 1." (from the ordinance text)
- "Torres voted against the measure." (from the roll call vote)

**Dollar amounts from official documents**
- "The contract is worth $3.2 million." (from the executed contract)
- "The city's general fund reserve is $8 million." (from the audited ACFR)

**Dates and document facts**
- "The meeting was held March 11." (from the minutes)
- "The staff report was published March 8." (from the document itself)

**Names and titles in official capacity**
- "City Manager Elena Rodriguez recommended approval."
- "Council Member James Torres abstained from the vote."

**Directly observed or directly verifiable facts**
- Text of an ordinance that can be read
- A vote tally that appears in the official record
- A figure that appears in an audited document

---

## Attribution required

These must be attributed to a named source or document:

**Reasons and motivations**
- Not: "The council rejected the contract because of concerns about oversight."
- Yes: "Council members citing oversight concerns voted against the contract." or "Three members said oversight concerns drove their votes."

**Staff analysis and recommendations**
- Not: "The project will cost $5 million."
- Yes: "The staff report estimated project costs at $5 million." (because estimates can be wrong)

**Any contested fact**
- Not: "The company failed to deliver the required report."
- Yes: "According to city records, the company had not filed the required annual report as of March 15."

**Statements and positions**
- Not: "The developer said the project would create 200 jobs."
- Yes: Exactly that — this is already attributed, and it should stay attributed (not stated as established fact)

**Projections and estimates**
- Not: "The city expects to save $400,000."
- Yes: "The city finance department projected savings of $400,000, according to the budget analysis."

## Levels of attribution: examples

**Strongest (official document)**:
"The contract, which the council approved in a 5-2 vote, is worth $3.2 million, according to the executed agreement filed with the city clerk."

**Document with source qualifier**:
"The city's pension fund had a funding ratio of 68 percent as of June 30, the most recent actuarial report shows."

**Staff document**:
"Staff recommended approval, citing the company's experience managing similar facilities in two neighboring cities."

**Individual statement at a public meeting**:
"Council Member Torres said at Tuesday's meeting that the city had not adequately studied the long-term costs of privatization."

**Individual statement outside a meeting**:
"Torres said in an interview Wednesday that he planned to request a full independent review."

**Anonymous/background source**:
"A city employee familiar with the contract negotiations, speaking on condition of anonymity because they were not authorized to discuss ongoing procurement, said the city received only one responsive bid."

## Indirect vs. direct quotation: when to use each

**Use a direct quote** (verbatim, in quotation marks) when:
- The speaker's exact words carry weight beyond the information (a formal commitment, a striking phrase, an unusual admission)
- Paraphrase would reduce or distort the precision or passion of the statement
- The words themselves are the subject (a council member's exact characterization of a policy)

**Use indirect quotation** (paraphrase, no quotation marks) when:
- The information matters but not the precise wording
- The statement is too long to quote in full
- The speaker used jargon that needs translation
- Multiple similar statements can be combined ("Several residents said they worried about traffic and noise")

**Example: direct vs. indirect from the same statement**

What the council member actually said: "I've sat on this body for eleven years, and I've never seen a staff report so thin on alternatives. We're being asked to approve a $3 million commitment based on three paragraphs."

Direct quote (appropriate — the specific, vivid criticism is the news):
> "I've sat on this body for eleven years, and I've never seen a staff report so thin on alternatives," Torres said.

Indirect quote (appropriate for a shorter summary):
> Torres said the staff report offered inadequate analysis to justify a $3 million commitment.

---

## Attribution placement

**Attribution at the end** is the default for simple statements:
> "The city plans to close two fire stations next year, Rodriguez said."

**Attribution in the middle** when the statement is long and the source needs to be clear before the reader reaches the end:
> "Rodriguez said the city had no alternative to the cuts — the general fund deficit left no other option."

**Attribution at the beginning** when the identity of the speaker is itself newsworthy:
> "Former City Manager David Chen, who resigned under pressure in 2024, said Tuesday that the pension shortfall had been growing for years before he left."

**Don't front-load every sentence with "according to."** Attribution rhythm matters. Vary placement. If every sentence in a paragraph begins with "According to the staff report," the writing is mechanical.

---

## Special cases

**Public meetings: no ground rules needed**
Everything said in open session is on-record. No agreement is necessary. "At the meeting" is sufficient attribution.

**Written public comments**
"In written comments submitted before the hearing, the neighborhood association said…"

**Emails obtained via public records request**
"In an email obtained by [publication] through a public records request, Rodriguez wrote…"

**Documents quoted verbatim**
"The staff report states: '[exact text]'" — use this formulation when the document's exact language matters (legal findings, formal characterizations).
