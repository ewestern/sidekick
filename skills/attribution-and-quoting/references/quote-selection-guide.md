# Quote Selection Guide

Criteria and examples for pulling quotes from meeting transcripts and written documents.

---

## The core test for a direct quote

A direct quote earns its place when the speaker's exact words are more powerful, precise, or authentic than paraphrase. Ask:
- Would a paraphrase convey this equally well? If yes, paraphrase.
- Does the specific language matter? If yes, quote directly.
- Does the quote advance the story beyond what surrounding paragraphs already convey? If no, cut it.

---

## Quote categories worth pulling

### 1. Statements of reasoning (the "why")

When a decision-maker explains the basis for a consequential vote or recommendation, the reasoning belongs in direct quotes — it is the official record of intent.

**Strong pull**:
> "We've been told for three years that this neighborhood needs a community center. This budget doesn't build it. This budget delays it again. I can't keep telling those families next year."
— Council member dissenting on the budget

*Why it works*: specific, passionate, represents a formal statement of the member's position that will be part of the public record.

**Weak pull** (paraphrase instead):
> "I think this is the right decision for the community."

*Why it fails*: generic; the paraphrase ("Torres said he supported the contract") conveys the same information.

### 2. Admissions and concessions

When an official or applicant acknowledges a problem, limitation, or prior error, direct quotes preserve the precision of the admission.

**Strong pull**:
> "The report was due in November. We did not submit it until March. I want to be clear that that's on this department, and we take responsibility for it."
— Department director, responding to audit finding

*Why it works*: an acknowledgment of responsibility that is precise, specific, and consequential. Paraphrase would dilute the directness.

### 3. Conflict and disagreement

When two officials or a body and an applicant are in direct conflict, direct quotes from both sides preserve the authentic character of the dispute.

**Strong pull**:
> "I've been doing business in this city for 20 years. I have never been treated this way."
— Applicant, after permit denial

**And the responding voice**:
> "The findings are in the record. The application didn't meet the standard. That's what the findings say."
— Planning commissioner

*Why both work*: the conflict is concrete; each voice represents a distinct, authentic position that paraphrase would generalize.

### 4. Formal commitments and statements of intent

When a governing body or official makes a formal commitment at a public meeting, the exact language is often legally or politically significant.

**Strong pull**:
> "The council directs staff to return with a report on full staffing options for the parks department no later than the first meeting in July."
— Chair, summarizing direction given to staff

*Why it works*: this is a formal direction; the exact language matters for accountability.

### 5. Distinctive voice or character

Occasionally a source says something in a way that is unmistakably theirs — a phrase that captures the political or human character of the moment in a way no paraphrase could replicate.

**Strong pull**:
> "You can't cut the bones out of a city and expect it to stand up."
— Longtime council member, opposing service cuts

*Why it works*: vivid, memorable, captures a position with authenticity. The metaphor is the point.

## Common weak quotes (paraphrase instead)

**Generic support**:
> "I think this is a great project and I'm proud to support it."
Paraphrase: Rodriguez said she supported the project.

**Restatement of the decision**:
> "After careful deliberation, the council has decided to approve the contract."
Paraphrase: The council approved the contract.

**Technical jargon without context**:
> "The CEQA mitigated negative declaration satisfies the requirements of Section 21080.1."
Paraphrase: Environmental review requirements were satisfied, staff said.

**Hedged, vague statements**:
> "We're looking at a number of different options and we'll be evaluating them as we go forward."
Paraphrase: Officials said they had not decided on a specific approach.

---

## Pulling from transcripts: practical method

1. **Read the full transcript for context** before selecting quotes. A fragment pulled without context may misrepresent the speaker's meaning.

2. **Identify the key moments**: vote explanations, public hearing highlights, cross-examination of staff, expressions of opposition or support from organized parties.

3. **Test each candidate quote** against the selection criteria above.

4. **Check for completeness**: does the quote say what the speaker actually meant? If the next sentence would change the meaning, the quote needs to include it or be paraphrased instead.

5. **Note the speaker's context**: was this a formal statement or an off-the-cuff remark? A formal vote explanation or a response to a colleague? Context determines weight.

---

## Cleaning up spoken quotes

Standard practice is to remove verbal fillers from spoken quotes for readability:
- False starts: "I, uh — what I'm saying is that…" → "What I'm saying is that…"
- Repetition: "We need — we really need — to address this" → "We really need to address this"
- Filler words: "um," "uh," "you know" — may be removed silently

**Not permissible**:
- Removing words that change the meaning
- Changing the verb tense to make a past statement present
- Combining statements from different parts of the meeting as if they were continuous
- Adding words that were not said

If a spoken quote cannot be made publishable without altering its meaning, paraphrase instead.
