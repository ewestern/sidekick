## When this knowledge applies

When pulling quotes from a meeting transcript or document, when determining
whether a claim requires attribution or can be stated as established fact,
or when assessing the appropriate attribution level for information from
different source types.

## Domain knowledge

### When attribution is required vs. when it is not

**No attribution needed** (established fact, directly observed):
- A vote tally recorded in official minutes: "The council voted 4-3 to approve the contract."
- The text of an adopted ordinance: "The ordinance takes effect June 1."
- A figure in an audited financial statement: "The city's general fund balance is $8.2 million."
- The date and location of a meeting
- Names, titles, and roles of officials in their official capacity

**Attribution required** (what someone said, decided, or interpreted):
- Any claim about why a decision was made: "according to the staff report" or "council members cited fiscal concerns"
- Any statement by a specific person: attribute to the speaker
- Any contested fact: if reasonable people could disagree, attribute to the source
- Any projection or estimate: "the finance department projects a $1.2 million shortfall"
- Any opinion or judgment, including staff recommendations: "staff recommended approval"

**The key distinction**: a reporter (or agent) states what they directly observed in the documents. Interpretations, explanations, and attributable claims require attribution to whoever made them.

### Public meetings: everything is on-record

Everything said in an open public meeting is on-the-record by definition.
No ground rules need to be established; the meeting is a public proceeding.

- Council members' statements during deliberation: on-record
- Public commenters' testimony: on-record (they chose to speak publicly)
- Staff presentations and responses to council questions: on-record
- Applicants' representations to the body: on-record

The only exception is closed session (executive session), which is legally
confidential. What was discussed in closed session is not on-record; what
was reported out of closed session in open session is on-record.

### Attribution ground rules (for source interviews, not documents)

These apply when interacting with human sources outside the meeting context:

**On the record**: the source is identified by name and title; everything
they say can be quoted directly and attributed. Default for all government
officials speaking in their official capacity.

**On background**: information can be used but not attributed to the source
by name. Often attributed to a role: "a city official" or "someone familiar
with the contract." Use sparingly; the attribution type should be established
explicitly before the source speaks.

**Off the record**: information cannot be used at all — it can only inform
other lines of inquiry. Not a common arrangement; avoid unless the source
insists and the information is sufficiently valuable to pursue independently.

**Not for attribution / background**: sometimes used interchangeably with "on
background"; the distinction is establishing before the conversation begins,
not after.

**The embargo**: information provided in advance of a public release, with
agreement not to publish until a specified date and time. Commonly used for
government budget releases, court decisions, or major reports. An embargo
accepted is a commitment — breaking an embargo damages a source relationship
and a publication's reputation.

### What makes a quote worth using

Potter on quotes and sound bites: a quote earns its place when it:
- Reveals authentic personality, passion, or conviction
- Says something the reporter could not say as effectively in paraphrase
- Expresses something that would be reduced or distorted by indirect quotation
- Captures the speaker's distinctive voice in a way that adds to the story

**A quote is not worth using when it**:
- Merely restates a fact that is better stated directly
- Uses jargon or bureaucratic language that will confuse readers
- Is so long that the reader loses track of the point
- Repeats what the story already established

Downing's rule: use quotes sparingly; they must advance the story. The average
news story does not need many direct quotes. One strong quote is worth ten weak ones.

### Selecting quotes from transcripts

When pulling quotes from a meeting transcript or written document, prioritize:

1. **Statements of reasoning**: why a member voted a particular way; what
   concerns were expressed; what the agency acknowledged
2. **Admissions and concessions**: a department head acknowledging a problem;
   an official confirming an allegation
3. **Expressions of commitment**: formal statements of intent or obligation
4. **Distinctive language**: a phrase that captures the political or emotional
   character of the moment
5. **Conflict or disagreement**: a dissenting council member explaining their
   vote; an applicant responding to opposition

**What not to pull**:
- Boilerplate procedural language ("I move to approve the consent calendar")
- Uninformative affirmations ("I agree with what my colleague said")
- Technical jargon without context
- Very long statements that would require extensive paraphrase anyway

### Paraphrase vs. direct quote

Use a **direct quote** (verbatim, in quotation marks) when the speaker's
exact words are important — because of their precision, their passion, or their
significance as a formal statement.

Use **indirect quotation** (paraphrase, no quotation marks) when:
- The content matters but not the exact wording
- The statement is too long to quote fully
- The speaker's language needs clarification for a general audience
- The statement was made in a format (email, written comment) where the
  words are less precise than a prepared statement

**Never**: alter the words inside quotation marks. If words must be omitted,
use ellipsis. If context must be added, use brackets. If the quote as stated
cannot be used accurately, paraphrase instead.

### Attribution precision

The level of attribution should match the precision of the claim:

- General policy statement: "the city" or "Springfield officials"
- Specific vote or action: the full governing body ("the city council voted")
- Statement by an individual: name and title ("City Manager Elena Rodriguez said")
- Anonymous/confidential: "a city official said" or "according to a person
  familiar with the contract, who spoke on condition of anonymity"

Over-attribution (attributing established facts) clutters writing. Under-
attribution (stating contested claims as facts) is inaccurate. Calibrate
the attribution level to the claim being made.

## Gotchas

**Public-record documents are not quotes from people.** A staff report says
something; the city manager who signed it said something. These are different
attributions with different weight. "According to the staff report" is accurate;
"the city manager said" is accurate only if attributing a statement in the
report to its named author.

**Attribution does not mean endorsement.** Attributing a claim to a source
does not mean the claim is true — it means that source made the claim. The
reader understands the distinction. A quote from a developer claiming their
project will create 200 jobs does not mean it will.

**Cleaning up quotes.** Removing false starts and verbal fillers ("um," "uh,"
"you know") from a spoken quote for readability is standard practice. Removing
or changing words that alter meaning is not. The line is clarity vs. accuracy.
