## When this knowledge applies

When assessing whether a document, event, or pattern warrants a flag,
a trend note, a brief, or a full story. When prioritizing across multiple
competing story candidates. When deciding whether a developing situation has
reached a threshold that warrants surfacing to editorial.

## Domain knowledge

### The kill/pursue/flag framework

Every potential story sits at one of three editorial dispositions:

**Pursue**: the development is significant, timely, and adequately sourced.
It warrants a story — now. The story needs a lead, a central development,
supporting detail, and context. It can be drafted.

**Flag**: the development is significant but incomplete. Something is
happening that warrants editorial attention, but the picture is not yet
complete — a key document is missing, the outcome is pending, or cross-
beat context is needed. Flag it; watch it; return to it when the missing
element arrives.

**Trend note**: a pattern is emerging that may become a story, but no
single development is yet significant enough to pursue alone. Document the
pattern, accumulate evidence, and determine whether it crosses the story
threshold over time.

**Kill**: not every interesting development is a story. Routine activity —
consent calendar approvals, standard renewals, procedural votes — does not
need editorial attention. Kill efficiently; the capacity to kill quickly
is what makes pursuing the right stories possible.

The test for kill vs. flag: would a reasonable editor, given the same
information, find this worth knowing about? If yes, flag it. If no, kill it.

### What makes something story-worthy vs. notable-but-routine

**Story-worthy** (strong candidate for pursue):
- A decision that changes something in the material world for identifiable people
- A contested outcome — a close vote, organized opposition, public controversy
- A disclosure that reveals something previously unknown (financial, personnel,
  contractual)
- An action that contradicts a prior position or commitment
- An expenditure or commitment above a material threshold with unusual
  circumstances (sole-source, no-bid, emergency, waiver of review)
- An absence or failure where presence was expected and required

**Notable but routine** (candidate for trend note or kill):
- Standard approval of a routine contract renewal without material changes
- Unanimous consent calendar approval of expected agenda items
- Acknowledgment of a report without action
- Procedural continuation of a matter to a future meeting
- Personnel actions within normal channels (hiring, promotion, reassignment)

The distinction is not always obvious. Use news values as the tiebreaker:
if an item scores on two or more news value criteria (timeliness + impact,
controversy + prominence, oddity + dollar threshold), that combination often
crosses the story threshold.

### The news budget

The news budget is the set of stories competing for a publication's attention
and space on a given day or cycle. Editorial judgment is partly an exercise
in resource allocation: given what else is happening, does this item warrant
the effort of development?

Potter: the editorial workflow includes:
- **Assignment**: what stories should be developed?
- **Budgeting**: which of the in-development stories makes this cycle?
- **Presentation decisions**: how prominently should a story be displayed?

For this pipeline, the equivalent question is: given the volume of documents
processed and the patterns emerging, which developments warrant flagging to
editorial attention — and in what order of priority?

**Priority ordering heuristic**:
1. Time-sensitive: decision pending, meeting imminent, deadline approaching
2. High-stakes: large dollar amounts, many people affected, significant
   policy change
3. Novel: something genuinely new, not a continuation of a known pattern
4. Confirmatory: a development that validates or contradicts a prior suspicion

### Distinguishing a flag from a trend note

**A flag** is a specific development at a specific moment that warrants
editorial attention now. It relates to a discrete event: a vote, a contract
award, a document filing. The basis for the flag is clearly articulable.

**A trend note** is a pattern observed across multiple data points over time.
It may not yet have a singular event to anchor it, but the accumulation of
evidence makes it worth tracking. Trend notes become stories when:
- A discrete event crystallizes the pattern (a vote that exemplifies the trend)
- The pattern reaches statistical or contextual significance
- A source confirms the trend in an attributable statement
- The pattern produces a concrete, visible outcome

**A flag without a news peg is a trend note.** If you cannot say "here is the
specific thing that happened, and here is why it matters now," you have a
trend note, not a flag.

### The significance threshold

Not every anomaly is significant. Not every contract is a story. The
significance threshold is the combination of factors that makes a development
worth editorial investment.

For most local government news:
- A single-source claim with no corroboration is below the threshold
- A documented action with context and a clear impact crosses the threshold
- A pattern with multiple data points and a plausible causal mechanism crosses
  the threshold

The threshold should be consistent: if a $200,000 sole-source contract is
flagged, a $500,000 sole-source contract from the same period should also be
flagged. Inconsistent thresholds create coverage gaps that become defensible
only in retrospect.

### Coverage balance

A beat should cover across the range of what the governing body does, not
only the most dramatic items. Consistent coverage of routine activity creates
the context within which anomalies become visible. An agency that never makes
news for routine business, then suddenly produces a scandal, was probably
producing signals that the coverage missed.

**Balance does not mean false equivalence**: a unanimous vote and a 4-3 vote
are not equally newsworthy simply because both are votes. Balance means
covering the range of consequential actions, not treating all actions as
equally consequential.

## Gotchas

**Novelty ≠ significance.** Something can be unusual without being important.
A council member's unusual vote on a minor item is odd; it may not be
significant. Oddity is one news value factor, not a sufficient basis for
a story on its own.

**Frequency ≠ significance.** A practice that happens repeatedly may be
routine or may be a recurring problem. The frequency of consent calendar
approvals does not make them newsworthy; an unusual pattern within those
approvals might.

**The absence of a story is also an editorial decision.** Choosing not to
flag something is itself a judgment. The pipeline should be able to explain,
for any document processed, why it was or was not flagged — not just that it
was processed.

**Context from prior coverage is part of the threshold calculation.** A
development that would be unremarkable in isolation may cross the threshold
because it connects to a pattern documented in prior coverage. The beat
agent's persistent memory is the mechanism for applying this context.
